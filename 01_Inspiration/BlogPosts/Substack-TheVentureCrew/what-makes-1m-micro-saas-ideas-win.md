##### **START WITH**

Google Cloud‚Äôs 2025 [DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment)
report](https://blog.google/technology/developers/dora-report-2025/) just
dropped, and it‚Äôs the clearest evidence yet that AI has fundamentally changed
the way software is built. Nearly 5,000 technology professionals across the
world were surveyed, and the findings show massive adoption ‚Äî but also some
surprising paradoxes.

Here‚Äôs what you need to know:

**1\. AI adoption is now mainstream**

  * 90% of software professionals (developers, PMs, engineers) now use AI in their workflows ‚Äî up 14% from last year.

  * Median usage: 2 hours a day spent with AI tools.

  * 65% report heavy reliance on AI, with 20% saying they rely on it ‚Äúa lot‚Äù and 8% ‚Äúa great deal.‚Äù

Translation: AI is no longer a sidekick ‚Äî it‚Äôs part of the daily toolkit for
nearly every developer.

**2\. The clear benefits**

  * Productivity: 80%+ say AI boosted how much they can ship.

  * Code quality: 59% report better-quality code as a direct result of AI use.

  * Throughput: Teams adopting AI are releasing more software and applications ‚Äî reversing last year‚Äôs mixed results.

In short, AI isn‚Äôt just helping individuals; it‚Äôs starting to improve
organizational velocity too.

**3\. The ‚Äútrust paradox‚Äù**

Even though AI is widely adopted, trust lags behind:

This paradox shows up in practice: developers use AI heavily because it‚Äôs
useful, but they don‚Äôt fully rely on it for final decisions. AI is a
productivity enhancer, not a replacement for human judgment.

**4\. AI as ‚Äúmirror and multiplier‚Äù**

The report introduces a critical idea: AI reflects the state of your team.

  * In cohesive teams, AI amplifies efficiency.

  * In fragmented teams, AI highlights weaknesses ‚Äî faster delivery, but also faster exposure of flaws.

This is why some orgs see AI as transformative, while others see only chaos.

**5\. The seven developer archetypes**

DORA moved beyond simple metrics this year and mapped seven team archetypes,
from ‚ÄúHarmonious high-achievers‚Äù to ‚ÄúLegacy bottlenecks.‚Äù These profiles show
how AI interacts with team culture and workflows. Big insight: organizational
health determines AI ROI.

**6\. The blueprint: DORA AI Capabilities Model**

Adoption alone isn‚Äôt enough. The report lays out seven essential capabilities
that drive meaningful AI impact: a blend of technical practices (like CI/CD,
data integration, and testing) and cultural factors (like feedback loops,
psychological safety, and cross-functional collaboration).

Put simply: AI won‚Äôt fix broken systems. To get the promised productivity
boost, orgs must evolve both their tools and culture.

Aventis Advisors [released](https://aventis-advisors.com/wp-
content/uploads/2025/09/SaaS-Valuation-Multiples-2015-Q3-2025.pdf) a new
analysis of SaaS valuation trends across public markets and private M&A from
2015 to 2025.

  * Growth slowdown: Median revenue growth hit 31% during COVID but has since dropped to 12.8% in Q2 2025, putting downward pressure on valuations.

  * Profitability push: Median EBITDA margins reached 7.5% in 2025, with investors rewarding efficient, cash-generating SaaS companies.

  * Rule of 40: Median score fell to 23%, but each 10-point improvement can add about 1.5√ó to revenue multiples, making it a critical benchmark.

  * Private M&A: More stable than public markets, with a long-term median around 4.7√ó EV/Revenue. Multiples dipped to 2.9√ó in 2024, but rebounded to 5.7√ó in 2025, mostly on larger $50M+ transactions.

  * Valuation drivers: Scale, durable growth, and defensible AI integrations are commanding premiums, while smaller SaaS firms with high burn face tougher scrutiny and tighter deal terms.

The SaaS boom-and-bust cycle has reset expectations. Going forward,
efficiency, scale, and AI defensibility‚Äînot just top-line growth‚Äîwill define
premium valuations.

##### **SOMETHING MORE**

Micro-SaaS = a focused SaaS product with pointy features for a very specific
customer. Pat [studied
dozens](https://docs.google.com/document/d/18sH0AV_s0vR_kbFGhWLCkgua6xcMwqCM9qU1UmjZbN4/edit?tab=t.0)
of case studies and found common patterns behind the winners:

  1. **Ride trends or shifts**

     * Growing trend ‚Üí e.g. waitlist software (ReferralHero, $18K MRR).

     * Missed opportunity ‚Üí Amazon FBA tools (Gorilla ROI, $40K MRR).

     * Change in the world ‚Üí rise of personal brands ‚Üí Tweet Hunter ($220K MRR & acquired).

  2. **Build on large platforms**

     * Shopify (2M merchants), Amazon FBA (1.5M sellers), Twitter (100M+ businesses).

     * Example: WideBundle, a Shopify plugin for product bundling, $55K MRR.

  3. **Pointy solutions**

     * Do one thing extremely well ‚Üí not an all-in-one.

     * Example: Testimonial ‚Üí embed video testimonials, $83K MRR.

  4. **Serve a narrow customer base**

     * CyberLeads: only digital agencies targeting VC-backed startups ($40K MRR).

     * Gorilla ROI: not all FBA sellers, only spreadsheet/data-heavy ones.

  5. **Solve painful business problems**

$1M micro-SaaS ideas aren‚Äôt random ‚Äî they win because they‚Äôre simple, built on
big platforms, serve specific customers, and solve money/time problems. Start
small, ship fast, and let the pointy feature lead you to growth.

Most founders overthink launches. They keep tinkering, waiting for ‚Äúperfect,‚Äù
and end up stuck in stealth. Claire Butler, Figma‚Äôs first marketer,
[says](https://review.firstround.com/how-to-launch-your-startup-out-of-
stealth/) the opposite is true: momentum beats perfection.

Here‚Äôs her framework for stepping out of stealth without stalling:

**1\. Set a date, even if it feels early**

  * Don‚Äôt wait for every feature to be done‚Äîjust reach ‚Äúcritical mass.‚Äù

  * Figma launched without multiplayer (its core feature) to avoid stagnation.

  * Rule of thumb: if you‚Äôre not slightly embarrassed, you‚Äôve waited too long.

**2\. Create three forcing-function artifacts**

  * **Website** : One homepage + about/careers. Define tone, positioning, CTA. (Waitlist vs sign-up vs talk to sales?)

  * **Announcement post** : Founder-written narrative ‚Üí why you built it, why now, where it‚Äôs headed.

  * **Social post** : Distill it all into a single screenshot, video, or punchy tagline. If it feels fluffy, rewrite until it‚Äôs authentic.

**3\. Pick launch channels wisely**

  * Focus where your ICP already hangs out (Reddit, Discord, LinkedIn, Twitter/X).

  * Use founder accounts to amplify first‚Äîbrand handles start from zero.

  * Press is optional. Unless tied to funding, don‚Äôt waste time chasing exclusives.

**4\. Seed amplification before launch**

  * Build a list: friends, beta users, influencers, your network.

  * Tell them when launch is, what you‚Äôll need, and share links day-of.

  * Momentum spreads outward from your circle‚Äîplan it, don‚Äôt wing it.

**5\. Remember: launch ‚â† finish line**

  * Monitor replies, engage, and bookmark supporters for ongoing community building.

  * Don‚Äôt obsess over metrics too soon‚Äîtrack impressions, traffic, sign-ups, but focus on vibes + momentum.

  * A flashy launch ‚â† product-market fit. The real game starts after.

A launch is a forcing function, not a final exam. Prep your date, artefacts,
channels, and amplification. Then get out there; momentum matters more than
perfection.

Most founders talk about growth. Few can clearly show how each new customer
makes or loses them money. That‚Äôs why running a unit economics model early
matters; it tells you if your business will compound or collapse.

Here‚Äôs how to use the sheet I‚Äôve shared:

1\. Map your revenues

  * Enter your average revenue per unit (subscription, fee, or transaction).

  * Decide if it‚Äôs recurring (monthly/annual) or one-time.

  * Pro tip: if you don‚Äôt know exact numbers yet, start with estimates and refine over time.

2\. Add cost of sales (COGS)

  * Enter costs as a % of revenue (hosting, delivery, support).

  * The sheet calculates your gross margin per unit automatically.

3\. Plug in churn + growth assumptions

  * Set billing cycle (monthly = 1, annual = 12).

  * Add churn % (customers who drop each cycle).

  * Add expected annual margin growth (e.g., 5%).

  * The model uses these to estimate the average customer lifetime.

4\. Add acquisition + retention costs

5\. Review key outputs

  * LTV (Lifetime Value): how much a customer is worth over their lifecycle.

  * LTV/CAC ratio: >3 is usually healthy.

  * CAC payback period: months it takes to earn back the acquisition spend.

  * Cumulative cash flows: show when you turn profitable per customer.

6\. Stress-test scenarios

Why this matters

  * Investors use this as a quick sanity check.

  * It forces you to confront pricing, churn, and acquisition head-on.

  * A business with shaky unit economics will break no matter how good the growth story sounds.

Recently, Brett Adcock, the founder of Figure Robot, shared a tweet in which

  1. Outbound cold email scales several orders of magnitude better than referrals - you can find 99.9% of anybody‚Äôs email.

  2. It‚Äôs a challenge finding somebody who will stick their neck out to refer you. People rarely want to use their political capital referring you to a *highly important person* in their network, therefore at best you‚Äôre left with lousy double opt-in email.
