##### **üìú DEEP DIVE**

These days, if you scroll through Twitter or LinkedIn, you‚Äôll see a familiar
story:  
A startup goes viral. A launch video racks up 1 million views. The founder
posts screenshots of a 10,000-person waitlist. Investors start circling.

I‚Äôve reviewed dozens of these startups for potential angel investment. Many
pitch impressive surface metrics, traffic spikes, social shares, thousands of
signups.

But the moment I ask a few simple questions, most of the answers are either
vague or missing altogether.

> Because here‚Äôs the truth: not all traction is created equal.

What feels like momentum is often just noise.

  * A viral launch might bring attention for a day, but attention doesn‚Äôt mean users.

  * A huge waitlist doesn‚Äôt mean people will show up.

  * And early revenue doesn‚Äôt mean the business is working.

This guide is about cutting through all that. These are six of the most common
vanity metrics startups use and what matters instead.

If you‚Äôre a founder, this will help you build trust through real signals. If
you‚Äôre an investor, it‚Äôll help you spot red flags before you write a check.

  1. **The viral launch trap**

Viral moments feel like a win. A product video hits 100,000 views. The website
crashes from traffic. Thousands of users sign up overnight.

But virality is fleeting. The real question isn‚Äôt how many people saw it, it‚Äôs
how many people came back.

Ask instead:

  * How many of those signups became active users?

  * What‚Äôs the 7-day and 30-day retention rate?

  * Are users returning without being nudged?

The signal isn‚Äôt in the spike ‚Äî it‚Äôs in the slope after the spike.

**2\. Revenue that isn‚Äôt repeatable**

Some startups start with early revenue, which looks great on paper. But when
that revenue comes from:

  * The founder‚Äôs connections

  * A one-off enterprise deal

  * Steep discounts to close deals fast

‚Ä¶it can give a false sense of product-market fit.

What matters more:

  * Are customers coming from diverse, scalable channels?

  * Are they paying full price?

  * Are they sticking around long enough to cover CAC?

Early revenue without repeatability is just noise.

**3\. Inflated waitlists**

A 10,000-person waitlist looks impressive. But how was it built?

If the list was grown through gift card giveaways, viral loops, or referral
bribes, it‚Äôs often low-intent and low-conversion.

Real signal comes from:

  * Conversion rate from waitlist to paid usage

  * Activation rate within 7 days of launch

  * Engagement metrics of early users

You don‚Äôt need a big list. You need a hungry one.

**4\. Niche fans ‚â† , broad market**

Some products gather passionate early adopters, indie hackers, communities on
Reddit, or hobbyists who love trying new tools.

That‚Äôs a great starting point. But niche love doesn‚Äôt always translate to
scalable demand.

You want to see:

  * Usage spreading beyond the founder‚Äôs inner circle

  * Demand from users in adjacent verticals

  * Growth without constant hand-holding

A tiny, obsessed fan base is encouraging. But market validation comes when
strangers start using and recommending the product, without being nudged.

**5\. The enterprise pilot illusion**

Startups selling to enterprises love to highlight pilot programs with big
brands. But corporate pilots are easy to land and even easier to lose.

Many never convert into real contracts.

Look for:

  * Pilot-to-paid conversion rates

  * Renewal rates after 6‚Äì12 months

  * Growth in usage or seat expansion over time

A pilot is a foot in the door. Real traction is when the product becomes
embedded in the customer‚Äôs workflow, when churning becomes painful.

**6\. Fundraising ‚â† product-market fit**

Raising from a top-tier VC can create momentum. But capital raised doesn‚Äôt
equal value created.

Some founders are simply excellent storytellers. They can pitch. They can sell
the vision. And that‚Äôs a skill worth admiring.

But it doesn‚Äôt guarantee a great product.

Instead, look at:

  * Organic growth driven by word of mouth

  * Unprompted referrals from customers

  * Metrics that show depth of usage (not just breadth)

> The best companies often don‚Äôt look loud in the beginning. But they have
> **consistent, compounding usage** that speaks for itself.

Remember, Vanity metrics create hype. But long-term value comes from
retention, engagement, expansion, and referrals.

Don‚Äôt just celebrate the first spike. Study what happens after it. Because
true traction doesn‚Äôt just show up, it sticks around.

Sometimes product inspiration comes from users. This time, it came from a

[Sheet music platform Soundslice](https://www.soundslice.com/sheet-music-

> Confused, co-founder Adrian Holovaty checked ChatGPT himself‚Ä¶ and discovered
> something wild:
>
> The AI was confidently telling users that Soundslice supported ASCII tab
> imports, a feature they never offered.

Rather than ignore the confusion, Holovaty and team leaned in. They shipped
it. A hallucinated feature became reality.

Here‚Äôs what makes this fascinating:

  * **The market was already acting** as if the feature existed. The demand was real, even if the origin wasn‚Äôt.

  * **It forced a product dilemma:** Should you build something just because an AI model said you do?

  * **Their choice?** Embrace it. Help users. And turn a false assumption into a real workflow.

It‚Äôs one of the first real cases of AI shaping a product roadmap by mistake.

> And it begs the question: When AI creates expectation at scale‚Ä¶ does that
> change what you should build?

A roadmap led by hallucination, but backed by demand. ([Original Story
