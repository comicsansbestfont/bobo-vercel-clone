##### **TODAY‚ÄôS DEEP DIVE**

‚ÄúBefore you set out in fundraising mode, understand that -

`fundraising is a sales & marketing process and needs to be managed. Somehow
many first-time founders equate ‚Äúsales‚Äù with something that is beneath them.`

As a founder, you need to understand that -

> ‚ÄúAn investors job is to deploy capital and make a return. If you _truly_
> believe that you, your company and your products are exceptional and your
> company will be valuable then you‚Äôre actually doing them a FAVOR by helping
> them invest in your startup.
>
> If you don‚Äôt believe in your bones that you‚Äôre amazing then it‚Äôs no wonder
> you don‚Äôt want to sell them on making the investment.‚Äù

**Like any sale, you first need to plan your ‚Äúprospects‚Äù** and qualify whether
or not they‚Äôd be a good fit for your product ‚Äî an investment in your company.
You need to figure out how much time to spend with each prospect and you need
to rigorously manage your time and the calendar. This is where most founders
err.

Most founders prepare a deck, ask a few friends and investors whom to meet,
get a few introductions and just wing it.

As a result founders often meet the wrong investors, and waste time on those
who ask for more information.

The typical VC process is as follows:

`They say there are three rules in property: Location, location, location. In
sales there are also three rules: Qualify, qualify, qualify.`  

Your entire process should be about ‚Äútesting‚Äù whether your prospect has:

If an investor isn‚Äôt engaging then they‚Äôre not suddenly going to get a term
sheet. The surest sign a fund-raising process has stalled is when you aren‚Äôt
getting follow-up meetings or hearing from the VC or hearing from friends that
they got a phone call or email asking about you.

If engagement wanes you either need to move that VC to a lower priority or you
need to find ways to improve on any of these dimensions (obviously points 2 &
3 might mean you‚Äôre meeting the wrong person in the firm).

Moving a VC from an A to a B doesn‚Äôt mean they aren‚Äôt still your top pick, it
just means your chances are less likely and your extremely limited resources
should be allocated elsewhere.

> One of the most common question that every founder have during fundraising‚Ä¶.

Of course, there‚Äôs no exact number of VCs you should meet ‚Äî these are simply
guidelines.

For simplicity, assume you‚Äôve raised some money from angels or seed investors
and you‚Äôre either raising an A round or a B round of venture capital.

Start with a list of approximately 40 **qualified** investors. So qualified
investors mean - they‚Äôre all the right: Size, geography, industry focus,
capacity available and they have raised a new fund in the last 3‚Äì4 years so
you know **they have dry powder.**

If you‚Äôre raising a round where a new lead investor would invest $5 million
the VC fund must have no less than $100 million and if you‚Äôre looking for them
to write $15‚Äì20 million as the lead their fund realistically should be at
least $400 million. If you think you‚Äôll have shared leads fund sizes can be
slightly smaller but as a rough guideline assume most Seed/A/B VC funds
wouldn‚Äôt allocate more than 5% of their fund to a first-time investment.

In terms of stack ranking - force yourself to have no more than 8‚Äì10 ‚ÄúA‚Äôs,‚Äù
8‚Äì10 ‚ÄúB‚Äôs‚Äù and the balance of 20‚Äì24 should be ‚ÄúC‚Äôs.‚Äù

**An ‚ÄúA‚Äù is somebody** who would be likely to invest in a company like yours
and if chosen is somebody you‚Äôd be interested in working with. If you‚Äôre not
likely to get Sequoia then just because they‚Äôre an amazing firm doesn‚Äôt mean
they go on your A list. If you got a 3.6 GPA in high school, you might WANT to
go to Stanford, but it isn‚Äôt likely, so you spend more energy on schools
you‚Äôre more likely to get into. Same with VC.

**The A‚Äôs are the firms** you‚Äôre going to work hardest to research, hardest to
find high-quality introductions to and make the most effort to engage with. To
be clear ‚Äî your list never stays static. If you have a mediocre meeting with a
high-quality prospect and you don‚Äôt think they‚Äôre likely to lean in they drop
to a B or C.

Likewise, if a firm that you don‚Äôt think is your top pick suddenly starts
engaging and doing work and showing you love you might put them as an A
because having an offer is important.

There is some debate about whether you should ‚Äútest drive‚Äù with a few firms
before a wider process or whether you just begin the process. People who
believe the former believe that you should see the market demand before too
many people know you‚Äôre ‚Äúin the market.‚Äù There‚Äôs some truth in this. It‚Äôs such
a small industry that if you talk with too many investors people will hear
that you‚Äôre in the market and quickly know who has passed.

**You first take 2 meetings with ‚Äúsafety schools‚Äù** meaning somebody on your B
list and somebody on your C list unless you already have a very strong
relationship with somebody on your A list. **This gives you good practice for
your A meetings** and you will have a sense of some likely questions, comments
and concerns.

Then begin your process in earnest with up to 8‚Äì10 firms. These are the ones
that you want and that you also have a realistic possibility of landing.
**Keeping it to 8‚Äì10 helps you manage the public information flow** that will
be broader if you see 20 firms and also helps you prioritize resources. You
can hit a broader group in a few weeks once you know how you did with your
initial meetings or perhaps if you did well you can keep your aperture narrow.

One of the most important aims of a fund-raising process is to keep similar
firms at the same stage of your process. If you‚Äôre talking with too small of a
set and one leans in early and offers you a term sheet and you‚Äôre not sure
that it‚Äôs the firm you wanted to work with it is incredibly difficult to slow
them down and say, ‚ÄúWe need to finish our process‚Äù as you run the risk that
they feel played. An investor who doesn‚Äôt feel like there is a two-way
commitment will eventually walk and look for deals they perceive as a better
two-way fit.

Honestly - every VC want you to come to see **only** me because that means I
have no competition and have time to get to know you. But honestly, you should
do this before you‚Äôre raising. If we feel a mutual connection then VC will
make your life easier by offering you a term sheet before you‚Äôre even raising.

The first meeting is usually with 1‚Äì2 people within the VC firm. You might
start with a partner in the meeting or it might be a principal or associate.
In any event, this is a ‚Äúscreening meeting‚Äù or as I‚Äôve called on my graphics
an ‚Äúintroduction‚Äù to the firm. It‚Äôs hard but not _**that**_ hard to get a
first meeting for talented teams who hustle. It‚Äôs infinitely harder to get a
prompt* second meeting due to rigorous time management on behalf of a VCs. (*
VCs will often grant you a second meeting in 9‚Äì12 months to hear a progress
update).

Many VCs will appear to be super friendly in a first meeting because they are
there to learn and get to know you and there‚Äôs no upside to being an asshole.
Some VCs shared a detailed way why - your startup does not fit in their
thesis. When I was raising funding for my consumer tech startup - one of the
VCs sent me a friendly note why - my startup was not a perfect fit for their
thesis.

I point out that VCs are often friendly in the first meeting because I‚Äôve
heard hundreds of founders tell me their first meeting was great only to feel
ghosted when there is limited engagement following this meeting.

`There is a super simple way to know if a VC is engaged. If you get a second
meeting, a follow-up phone call or you know they‚Äôre doing actual work then
they‚Äôre engaged. No VC spends more time evaluating your company unless they
know that they at least have some interest.`

> _This is a sales process and your job is to look for ‚Äúbuying signals‚Äù ‚Äî
> remember: Qualify, qualify, qualify._

Other signs of engagement are: they ask you to meet with portfolio companies
(they want feedback on your product and you), they ask you to meet a
colleague, they set up a call to go through a product demo / financial walk-
through, they ask to speak with customers, etc.

It is NOT necessarily engagement if they ask you to send a bunch of financial
information under the guise of ‚Äúanalyzing your firm.‚Äù It drives me nuts but
many VCs ask for all of this because they figure more data is better than less
and they might as well have insight into how your numbers look. You can read
more on this data part in our previous newsletter write-up.

**If a VC ‚Äúghosts you‚Äù** (i.e. they told you it was a great meeting but then
they don‚Äôt respond to emails) DO NOT ASSUME it means they‚Äôre not engaged.
Sometimes ‚Äî best will in the world ‚Äî people just get busy. Your job is to push
politely until you either get a ‚Äúsoft no‚Äù or more engagement.

`If you fold simply because they haven‚Äôt responded to your last two emails you
won‚Äôt have success in business development, sales, press, recruiting ‚Ä¶
anything.`

Every important person with whom you want to do business will sometimes go
dark on you as self-preservation for other tasks they‚Äôre trying to complete.

Most entrepreneurs make the mistake of allocating too much time to taking new
meetings or spending time on the wrong investors simply because they‚Äôll keep
meeting with you. Don‚Äôt. Keep your eye focused first and foremost on any VCs
that are A‚Äôs and are in your ‚Äúanalysis‚Äù or ‚Äúdue diligence‚Äù phases. This is the
green and yellow portions of my graphic above which is specifically as a
reminder to you. Most entrepreneurs don‚Äôt put enough effort into these phases.

Sometimes engagement at the later stages seems to go dry. They haven‚Äôt said
‚Äúno‚Äù but they don‚Äôt seem to be spending a lot of time thinking about whether
to progress.

It should be obvious to you. At the end of the process is when they need to
decide not only whether they want to invest $5‚Äì10 million in your company and
take the personal risk of being wrong, but they‚Äôre also voting on how they
might spend a considerable amount of their time for the next 5‚Äì10 years and
nobody smart does this lightly.

Your job is to create reasons to spend more time with you and to draw them
into engaging because the more time they‚Äôre doing work, thinking about you,
spending time with you and getting their head around why this could be
exciting the more likely they will bring you into a partner meeting or make a
final commitment to you.

Some easy hacks to get in front of a VC again if your process stalls

  * Have them meet key team members they haven‚Äôt yet met ‚Äî particularly if these are people whom the VC would want to know regardless of whether or not they fund your company

  * Show demos of products that are yet to be released. This requires you to be disciplined and not sure if it is early in the process but a quick message to a VC that says, ‚ÄúI‚Äôd love to show you some cool new features we‚Äôve built in that we haven‚Äôt shown the market yet ‚Äî can I get 20 minutes to swing by‚Äù is a good way to engage.

  * Try to create new analyses on cohorts, future revenue projections, competitor reviews, pricing studies, etc. Any information that creates a compelling next meeting is worth doing. With some pre-planning, you might even know what information you show in your first or second meeting and what fragments you save for a follow-up meeting.

  * Another idea is to ask whether it‚Äôs ok to meet another team member of that VC in a 1‚Äì1 session to also show them your product. You can‚Äôt ask for a generic person ‚Äî it must be a named individual that has some reason for you to meet. But that‚Äôs a chance for you to ‚Äúland and expand‚Äù and build more fans inside the VC firm. It doesn‚Äôt have to be a partner ‚Äî every advocate on the inside is valuable.

Due diligence meetings are the hardest to secure because VCs of course know
that these follow-up meetings create obligations for them and if they‚Äôre
balancing five potential deals and haven‚Äôt decided whether or not you‚Äôre a fit
yet then they don‚Äôt meet again easily. As a result, many entrepreneurs take
the easy route of taking new first meetings because they‚Äôre easier to get,
easier to prepare for (you already have a deck) and they feel like progress.
Frankly, this is like running a sales campaign and when the last big push to
persuade four disparate departments to back you and it starts feeling
difficult, you instead start working on selling to different customers.

As dumb as it sounds, this is a very common playbook for entrepreneurs. The
bottom end of the funnel is hard. Damn hard. But I‚Äôd rather see your time and
energy go into creating new artefacts to share with your prospective VCs at
the bottom of the funnel than just purely taking too many new meetings.

In the earliest stages of this post, I mentioned that fund-raising is a ‚Äúsales
& marketing‚Äù process but I‚Äôve only spoken about sales. Marketing support is as
critical in a fundraising process as it is in a sales campaign. If you‚Äôre ever
been involved with enterprise marketing you know how important it is to have
marketing collateral and to have email drip campaigns to drive re-marketing
campaigns to prospects who showed interest but didn‚Äôt convert and to run PR so
that you stay on top of mind.

If you accept that these marketing techniques are critical in enterprise sales
then please understand that they are no less critical in fundraising. When you
plan out your fund-raising process you should dedicate some tasks on your
GANTT chart to marketing. When VCs are thinking about taking a second, third
or fourth meeting it doesn‚Äôt hurt that they saw an article about you in the
WSJ, Recode or TechCrunch. If you have a friend of the VC who is a customer of
your product or an existing investor in your company and they share news of
your company in their social feeds it helps to remind the VC that they need to
engage.

Every VC just like every consumer of any product likes to think that we aren‚Äôt
at all influenced by marketing but of course, any behavioural economist can
prove to you that we are. As a founder, use this basic knowledge to your
advantage.

Having pleaded with you to put more time into the bottom end of the funnel, I
do want to encourage you not to completely ignore the top end of the funnel.

In some cases VCs lean into a deal, do tons of work and seemingly get so
interested that they are about to submit a term sheet only to have them say
‚Äúno‚Äù at the last minute. Your lead on the deal was likely sincere in his or
her interest in you but possibly got shut down when seeking approval.

The problem with putting all of your eggs into this one basket is that if you
do get a ‚Äúno‚Äù then you don‚Äôt have a well-established pipeline of other
prospects who have already gotten through a meeting or two and you end up
having to go back to square one and you lose 6‚Äì8 weeks, which can be
existential for some startups.

It seems obvious that you shouldn‚Äôt count on one VC whose process seems to be
going well but I‚Äôve seen so many entrepreneurs do this that I want to
highlight it and remind you not to let this happen. Even if you are certain
that you‚Äôre about to get a term sheet you need to keep working a few names in
the top end of the funnel all of the way through a signed term sheet. Never
just assume that this will come through.

I know you‚Äôre likely tired at the end of a process but it‚Äôs important to
sprint through the finish line. Even if you DO get a term sheet there‚Äôs no
saying you‚Äôre going to like the terms and with no pipeline behind you you‚Äôll
likely feel pressured to just say ‚Äúyes.‚Äù

Fund-raising is a sales & marketing process in which the buyer is a VC and the
product is equity in your company.

Any great sales & marketing campaign begins with methodical planning and any
great process is run with rigorous time allocation on the most important
prospects.

Because many startup founders view ‚Äúrunning the business‚Äù as their only job
and view fund-raising as something that they‚Äôre forced to do every 18 months
it often doesn‚Äôt get the time, attention and resources it deserves. Indeed,
fund-raising in its own right won‚Äôt make you successful, but being successful
at fund-raising can give you a distinct advantage in the market against your
competitors who aren‚Äôt as good at getting financed as you or have to spend
more time in the market.

Plan accordingly. Fundraising is a year-round activity and never ends. Place a
small amount of your monthly time allocation to this task. Outside of fund-
raising periods, it should still be at least 15% of your time. It‚Äôs a large
part of the job of a successful CEO.
