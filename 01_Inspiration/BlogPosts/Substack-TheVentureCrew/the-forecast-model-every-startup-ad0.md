##### **üìú TODAY‚ÄôS DEEP DIVE**

Running a startup can feel like captaining a boat through a storm. You‚Äôre
blindfolded. The compass is broken. And you‚Äôre mostly guessing.

That‚Äôs where a financial forecast model comes in. First thing - it won‚Äôt tell
you the future. But it will help you see what‚Äôs coming, make smarter
decisions, and stretch your cash in the right direction.

If ‚Äúbuilding a financial model‚Äù sounds complex, overwhelming, or like
something you‚Äôll ‚Äúdo later‚Äù... good news: is it doesn‚Äôt have to be. We‚Äôve
created a straightforward forecast model template you can copy, customize, and

Let‚Äôs walk through why every founder (yes, even early-stage) should have one
and what to focus on.

  1. **Cash is oxygen**

Forget ‚Äúcash is king.‚Äù For startups, cash is air.

Without it, you‚Äôre done. A forecast model helps you project income and
expenses across time, so you‚Äôre not surprised when the runway gets tight.
Instead of reacting when you're gasping, you‚Äôre making moves ahead of time.

  2. **Speak the language of money**

Investors. Board members. Loan officers. Even vendors.

They all want to see that you‚Äôve got a handle on your finances. A forecast
model helps you show not just say that you‚Äôre building with a plan. It proves
you‚Äôre not winging it, even if it feels like that some days.

  3. **Get better at saying no**

Startups are full of decisions that sound exciting: ‚ÄúLet‚Äôs ramp up ads!‚Äù or
‚ÄúLet‚Äôs hire three more engineers!‚Äù

But can you afford to do that and still build the product? A forecast model
helps you understand tradeoffs, prioritize resources, and stay focused on the
metrics that matter.

  4. **Know what really moves the needle**

Whether your goal is acquisition, profitability, or that next funding round
your forecast helps you zoom out and see what truly impacts your outcomes.

For example: improving retention by just 5% might move revenue way more than
launching that new feature you‚Äôre debating.

Let‚Äôs break it down into five core sections you should focus on, especially in
the early stages:

  1. **Customer acquisition**

Think about how you attract new customers. Break it into clear channels like:

  * Performance marketing: Ads, paid campaigns, etc. (Track CAC!)

  * Sales team productivity: Deals won per rep.

  * Organic growth: SEO, word of mouth, referrals.

Start simple, don‚Äôt overcomplicate with 10 variables per channel. Use broad
assumptions, then refine with real data over time.

  2. **Customer retention**

Acquiring a customer means nothing if you can‚Äôt keep them. Your model should
reflect how long customers stay, and when they tend to churn.

Use cohort analysis or a basic monthly churn rate. Ask:

Retention is also your best indicator of product-market fit.

  3. **Revenue**

Revenue should be tied directly to customer activity:

  * For SaaS, it‚Äôs subscription price √ó active customers.

  * For marketplaces, it could be volume √ó take rate.

Start with average revenue per user (ARPU), and evolve into more granular
views as you scale.

  4. **Expenses**

Your biggest expense? People. Use a headcount plan to map out salaries, hiring
timelines, and team functions. Other key categories include:

  * COGS: Hosting, inventory, services delivered.

  * Marketing spend: Performance + brand.

  * Tools & software: Scale with team size and complexity.

  * Office, travel, services: Keep it lean unless proven otherwise.

Don‚Äôt default to ‚Äú% of revenue‚Äù unless you have no better data. You control
your budget, make it intentional.

  5. **Cash flow + runway**

At the end of the day, it‚Äôs about how long your cash lasts. Forecasting your
runway (how many months you can operate before running out of money) helps
guide:

  * Fundraising timelines

  * Hiring plans

  * Growth investments

Always stress-test with multiple scenarios:

  * Best-case (aggressive growth)

  * Base-case (reasonable assumptions)

  * Worst-case (conservative path)

**Tips to get started (without burning out)**

  * Start simple. This isn‚Äôt a Wall Street IPO model. It‚Äôs your startup‚Äôs guiding tool.

  * Update monthly. Compare forecast vs. actuals. Adjust your assumptions.

  * Don‚Äôt aim for perfection. Aim for utility. It‚Äôs better to have a rough, usable model than a flawless one that sits unopened.

  * Think of it as a living document. Not a one-time exercise.

**Remember your forecast model = your strategy, in numbers**

You don‚Äôt build a model because investors ask. You build it to understand your
own business better. To make smarter decisions. To align your team. To spot
problems early and act on them.

‚ÄúThe real magic happens when you strike a balance between planning and doing.‚Äù

##### **üìÉ QUICK DIVES**

Lovable is reportedly the fastest-growing startup in the world right now. Its
promise? Turn simple prompts into high-fidelity, interactive product
prototypes. Describe your app idea in a sentence or two, and Lovable builds a
functional UI around it no coding, no design tools.

It‚Äôs the kind of magical AI experience that spreads fast. But the more you dig
in, the more you notice something strange.

Subtle Software recently shared [an article
discussing](https://www.subtle.so/perverse-incentive-of-lovables-growth.html)
the same topic. Here are a few points and takeaways worth considering it‚Äôs a
valuable read for any founder building products.

The product is good but not great. It gets close, but often makes small
mistakes. UI elements misalign, interactions don‚Äôt quite match, and it usually
takes a few extra prompts to get things looking right.

That‚Äôs where the real business model kicks in.

Lovable‚Äôs free tier gives you just five prompts per day. And you‚Äôll probably
burn through them fast **not because you‚Äôre experimenting, but because the
tool doesn‚Äôt get it right on the first try**.

Which leads to an important insight that most people miss:

> There‚Äôs a perverse incentive: the less perfect the output, the more prompts
> you use, and the more likely you are to pay.

This doesn‚Äôt mean it‚Äôs intentional but it does raise questions. If the product
became too efficient, fewer people would hit the paywall. Slowing down the
path to revenue growth.

And yet, that friction combined with the delight of seeing your idea come to
life creates a powerful loop. People talk about it. Share it. Growth explodes.

**But is it sustainable?**

For many users, Lovable feels like a one-time thrill. You try it, show it off,
maybe even build a quick MVP. But then you hit limits:

  * It‚Äôs not always production-ready

  * You still need to clean up the UI

  * There‚Äôs no clear handoff into actual deployment

So retention becomes the big question.

A growing number of product people believe the real future of tools like
Lovable isn‚Äôt for indie builders it‚Äôs for agencies and product teams who want
to iterate faster, prototype collaboratively, and cut out weeks of back-and-
forth.

Imagine replacing a static slide deck with a live prototype. The team and the
client sit in the same room, making changes together. That‚Äôs a powerful
workflow shift and Lovable 2.0 is betting big on it.

Still, the competition is rising fast. Designers have mentioned tools like v0
as better alternatives. Dev-first platforms like Vercel may offer smoother
integrations. And larger players like GitHub or OpenAI could easily roll out
competing products with better polish and deeper ecosystems.

Here‚Äôs the takeaway:

  * **Text-to-app tools are magical, but still early.** They‚Äôre great for ideation, not yet great for production.

  * **Paywall design matters.** If users are paying because the tool makes mistakes, not because it delivers value that‚Äôs a long-term risk.

  * **The winners will be platforms, not demos.** The tools that become sticky will be the ones teams build into their actual workflow.

Lovable may be growing faster than any other startup‚Äîbut that kind of growth
comes with a catch. If users don‚Äôt stick, or if competitors catch up, that
rocketship can stall just as fast.

The excitement is real. But so is the churn risk.

Every founder has felt it: that sting of watching a hype-fueled startup with
no product and no revenue raise millions, while your cash-flow-positive,
growing business struggles to even get a meeting.

It feels unfair. And it is.

But there‚Äôs a deeper logic behind it. One that has little to do with how
‚Äúgood‚Äù your business is and everything to do with how venture capital works.

Recently, Steve Blank broke [down](https://steveblank.com/2025/07/01/why-
investors-dont-care-about-your-business/) why so many real businesses with
customers, revenue, and even profits often fail to attract VC attention. The
core idea? Venture capital isn‚Äôt built to fund good businesses. It‚Äôs built to
fund massive ones.

> Most founders misunderstand what investors are actually looking for. They
> assume that solid traction and profitability should be enough.

But venture capital follows a different playbook entirely.

Here‚Äôs what many founders miss:

  * **VCs are in the business of power-law outcomes.** They need 10x‚Äì100x returns to make [their math](https://theventurecrew.substack.com/i/155318590/steps-to-know-if-your-company-can-return-a-vc-fund) work. A steady-growing $20M company might be a great business but it‚Äôs not the billion-dollar rocket ship they‚Äôre hunting for.

  * **Your startup is a financial instrument.** The moment you raise venture money, your company becomes part of a portfolio. Investors aren't just betting on your growth they‚Äôre betting on a future exit (IPO, acquisition, or secondary sale) within 7-10 years.

  * **Perception drives value.** Especially now, when[ IPOs are rare and secondaries are a popular](https://www.cardumencapital.com/blog-posts/secondaries-reshaping-the-exit-landscape-for-vc-funds) liquidity path, what matters is how compelling your company looks to the next investor in line. The story you‚Äôre telling has to be big, bold, and believable.

This is also why certain sectors become hot overnight. Once a few top-tier
firms back AI companies, everyone wants in. The result?

  * Floods of capital chase a handful of ‚Äúhot‚Äù categories

  * Even questionable ideas get funded if they have the right narrative

  * Meanwhile, proven but ‚Äúunsexy‚Äù companies get ignored

> And secondaries have quietly changed the game. With fewer public exits, many
> investors are making money by selling their stakes to other funds. These
> aren‚Äôt new shares, they‚Äôre early investors cashing out while the company is
> still private.
>
> But this only works if the startup‚Äôs perceived value keeps rising so hype
> and optics matter more than ever.

So what can you do?

  * **Understand the game.** Your business may be amazing, but that doesn‚Äôt mean it‚Äôs venture-scalable. And that‚Äôs okay.

  * **Match your story to the investor.** Some funds chase trends. Others back overlooked gems. Know which one you‚Äôre pitching.

  * **Don‚Äôt assume they‚Äôll ‚Äúget it.‚Äù** You have to show them why your company can be massive and do it in a way that aligns with their timelines, incentives, and risk appetite.

Because once you take VC money, their model becomes your reality. If you don‚Äôt
fit what they‚Äôre looking for or don‚Äôt know how to frame your business in a way
that does you‚Äôll keep hearing ‚Äúno‚Äù for reasons that have nothing to do with
the actual quality of what you‚Äôre building.

And if you do have the kind of story they‚Äôre chasing? Now‚Äôs the time to go
big. Just remember: what they want isn‚Äôt a business. It‚Äôs a breakout.
