##### **üìú DEEP DIVE**

**Our controversial take:** A great fundraising process is only 20% about
pitching. The other 80% is all about organisation.

Yep, you heard that right.

Your pitch, the thing you‚Äôve spent weeks refining and rehearsing, is only a
small part of successful fundraising.

‚ÄúWhat the heck do y‚Äôall mean by the organisation?‚Äù - you, probably

If you‚Äôre talking to dozens (or even hundreds) of investors, keeping track of
who they are, what stage they‚Äôre at, and what you last discussed is impossible
to do from memory. That‚Äôs where an investor CRM (Customer Relationship
Management) system comes in.

Some founders use tools like HubSpot, Salesforce, or Pipedrive, but you don‚Äôt
need fancy software. A simple Google Sheet can do the job. What matters is
that it helps you track key details, follow-ups, and next steps, so no
potential investor falls through the cracks.

**Walking through an investor CRM**

This spreadsheet may look like a lot at first. But a CRM is way simpler than
you might think.

**Basic contact info**

To kick things off, add the names and email addresses of the investors that
you already know.

Then add the names and email addresses of prospective investors that you would
like to build stronger relationships with.

**Type of Investor**

In the next column, label what type of investor they are (angel investor,
venture fund, etc). This will help you tailor your messaging accordingly.

**Referrer**

The **referrer** is the person who introduced you to the investor listed in
your CRM.

So if ABC sent an email to introduce me to an investor, ABC is the referrer in
this example?  

This is crucial because your team can see where your leads are coming from.
Plus if the introduction is a mutually beneficial one, we get a chance to
reach out to the referrer again to say thanks.

Add their names in the next column and let‚Äôs keep moving.

**Stage**

What stage are they at in terms of considering an investment in your company?

These stages can be customized to your situation‚Ä¶ but for simplicity, let‚Äôs
define the stages that most funds used during our fundraising process.

  * **Lead** ‚Äì When someone offers to refer somebody new to you but you haven‚Äôt made contact yet.

  * **First Contact** ‚Äì This stage is the first time you both have been able to officially engage. Someone might have sent an email introduction and they may have reciprocated back saying, _‚ÄúYes, let‚Äôs meet!‚Äù_

  * **First Pitch** ‚Äì This is the first meeting you have with them to pitch your company.

  * **Due Diligence** ‚Äì This comes after the pitch when they‚Äôre considering investing. It‚Äôs normal for investors to ask to see more materials to help them make a decision.

  * **Soft Commit** ‚Äì This is when they are interested in investing and have given you a dollar amount they want to put towards your company. This is purely a verbal commitment, nothing official, but a positive sign to move them into the next stage.

  * **Signed** ‚Äì This is when the confetti drops from the ceiling because you have received a full commitment from this investor. Whooot!!! üéä

  * **Rejected and Ghosted** ‚Äì These are the stages where they reject your pitch or never return your messages. But remember, it‚Äôs not over yet. There‚Äôs still an opportunity to maintain the relationship or even invite them to your [biweekly/monthly investors update](https://theventurecrew.substack.com/i/152011320/how-to-write-monthlybi-weekly-investors-updates-email-template). They may convert into investors down the road.

**Committed**

If they have given you a verbal commitment of a certain amount they want to
invest, include this number in the **Committed** column.

Above, you can see three investors committing a combined total of $300,000 as
an example.

**Notes**

The **Notes** section is the most critical part of the CRM.

It summarizes what you talked about during your calls or emails. Add as many
notes as you can along with the dates of each interaction.

These notes are crucial because fundraising is a team sport.

Tracking everything helps your team understand the context of the
relationship. So when they interact with this person, they know where each
investor is in the process, and can pick up right where their teammate left
off.

**Closed and Next Contact**

Next, we have a **Closed** column to show how much money you have closed.  

This is your time to celebrate your hard work being paid off. Launch the
confetti. Take out a bottle of champagne. You deserve it.  

Ok, time to get back to work. The last part of the CRM is a **Next Contact**
column. This is a reminder for yourself on when you should reach out to this
person again.  

So if you're still in the due diligence or soft commit phases, set a clear
date here on when you should follow up.  

If people have committed, we recommend getting aggressive and following up
every two or three days. This shows that you‚Äôre committed to making this work
and are certain you have given them all the materials they need to feel
unblocked on making a decision.  

For the people who have rejected or ghosted you, you should keep reaching out.  

Remember a rejection is never truly a rejection until you get a hard ‚Äúno‚Äù.

So your investor CRM is a critical tool for successful fundraising.

  * All your contacts are in one place with detailed information

  * All the interactions your team has had with each person are tracked so everyone is on the same page

  * It tracks where everyone is in the fundraising process and gives reminders on when to follow up.

Start your investor CRM as soon as possible. Like, now.

There are paid CRM tools out there to track opens and clicks on emails. But to
keep things simple, we recommend starting with a basic spreadsheet.

##### **üìÑ QUICK WIN**

I‚Äôve been exploring Bedrock‚Äôs investment strategy lately, and their approach
is unlike that of most Silicon Valley firms.

Instead of chasing the ‚Äúnext big category‚Äù that everyone is talking about,
they actively hunt for what they call **narrative violations** , companies
that don‚Äôt fit the market‚Äôs current story.

**Why? Because in venture capital, popular narratives create overcrowded
markets:**

  * They attract too much capital

  * They spawn dozens of clones.

  * They create hype cycles where early returns are gone before most can get in

A narrative violation is the opposite. It‚Äôs when the consensus says this won‚Äôt
work, this market is dead, or this company will get crushed.

**Examples Bedrock highlights:**

  * 2010: ‚ÄúCleantech is dead‚Äù ‚Üí Tesla

  * 2011: ‚ÄúSocial gaming is a horrible business‚Äù ‚Üí Roblox

  * 2012: ‚ÄúDating apps are fads‚Äù ‚Üí Tinder

  * 2014: ‚ÄúUber will crush all competitors‚Äù ‚Üí Lyft

  * 2016: ‚ÄúConsumer is dead‚Äù ‚Üí Canva

  * 2020: ‚ÄúAI has been overhyped for a decade‚Äù ‚Üí OpenAI

When Bedrock invested in Lyft in 2012, the prevailing belief was that Uber‚Äôs
ruthlessness would wipe them out.

When they backed Rippling, the narrative was that HR tech was saturated.

When they invested in Vercel, open-source wasn‚Äôt seen as enterprise-friendly.

Geoff Lewis, Bedrock‚Äôs founder, built the firm on this principle in 2018 with
$127M AUM. Today, they manage around $2 billion and have backed over 50
companies, including OpenAI, Bitcoin, and Flock Safety, many of which were at
points when the consensus was betting against them.

**Why this works:**

  1. Immunity from clone wars ‚Äì When your business isn‚Äôt ‚Äúhot,‚Äù no one‚Äôs rushing to copy it.

  2. Systematic underestimation ‚Äì Investors and competitors overlook you, giving you more time to build.

  3. Longer runways ‚Äì Less capital chasing you means more breathing room.

Bedrock calls out a key danger in venture: the narrative mirage, when
investors see a small glimmer of success in a new category and immediately
inflate it into a ‚Äúonce-in-a-lifetime opportunity.‚Äù

Electric scooter sharing, daily deals, chatbots, all examples where billions
poured in, most companies died, and only one or two players survived.

So**,** Narratives can be inspiring, but they can also become shortcuts for
thinking. In venture, those shortcuts often mean missing the real
breakthroughs.

If you‚Äôre a founder, this should matter to you too.

Building something that violates the popular narrative might feel lonely at
first, no hype, no easy press, no VC buzz. But that‚Äôs exactly why you can win.
By the time the story catches up, you‚Äôre already too far ahead.

Don‚Äôt just follow the story everyone‚Äôs telling. Look for the story no one‚Äôs
telling yet.

Most products fail because they‚Äôre only slightly better than what exists. The
upgrade feels nice, but not enough to make people switch.

A Delta-4 product is different; it‚Äôs so much better that once people try it,
they never go back.

> The ‚Äú4‚Äù means that when the same group of users rates the old way and your
> way (1-10 scale), your product scores at least 4 points higher.
>
> Less than that, and you‚Äôve built an improvement, not a behaviour-shifting
> product.

**How to build one:**

  1. **Find a universally hated experience:** Look for something people complain about but have accepted as ‚Äújust how it is.‚Äù

     * Before Uber, getting a taxi meant finding one on the street, hoping it stopped, negotiating a price, and paying in cash.

     * Before Google Search, finding information meant browsing directories, clicking through slow pages, and often coming up empty.  

  2. **List every pain point:** Don‚Äôt stop at the obvious. Map the whole journey and capture all friction, time waste, uncertainty, hidden fees, manual steps, and trust issues.

     * Uber didn‚Äôt just solve ‚Äúfinding a ride.‚Äù They solved: locating a driver, knowing the fare upfront, tracking the route, paying without cash, and getting a receipt instantly.

     * Google didn‚Äôt just index the web. It made results instant, ranked by relevance, and accurate enough that you rarely clicked past the first page.  

  3. **Reimagine, don‚Äôt polish**  
Small tweaks don‚Äôt change behaviour. The biggest winners remove the core
friction entirely.

     * Netflix didn‚Äôt make video rental stores faster: it removed the store.

     * The iPhone didn‚Äôt add a better keyboard: it removed it, creating a touch interface.  

  4. **Measure your Delta before scaling**  
Survey 100 users of the old way: ‚ÄúRate your current experience from 1‚Äì10.‚Äù
Build your MVP, then have the same 100 rate your product. If the gap is +4 or
more, you‚Äôve hit Delta-4. If not, remove more friction.  

  5. **Push past ‚Äúgood enough‚Äù**  
Once you‚Äôve reached Delta-4, ask: ‚ÄúWhat would the 7-star version look like?‚Äù

     * Uber could add ‚Äúdriver greets you by name, with your favourite coffee in the cupholder.‚Äù

     * Google could anticipate your query and show the exact answer before you finish typing.

**Why this works:** A true Delta-4 product does three things:

  * Creates irreversible behaviour: once switched, users refuse to go back

  * Inspires loyalty: if you‚Äôre down, they‚Äôll wait rather than revert

  * Drives organic word-of-mouth: people can‚Äôt resist telling others

When you combine friction removal, instant value, and moments of delight, you
build a product that spreads without ads and is hard to disrupt.
