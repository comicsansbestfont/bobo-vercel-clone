##### **üìú TODAY‚ÄôS DEEP DIVE**

Most founders assume offering a free tier is the best way to drive product-led
growth, especially if you are a SaaS founder. Low friction, more users, bigger
top-of-funnel, it sounds like a smart strategy.

But in practice? Freemium quietly breaks more startups than it helps.

We‚Äôve explored this before, how to evaluate [whether freemium
fits](https://theventurecrew.substack.com/i/162108387/is-freemium-right-for-
your-saas-product-ask-these-questions) your business model. It‚Äôs a nuanced
decision, and getting it wrong can drain your resources without meaningful
conversion.

Recently, I came across this post by [Jason
Cohen](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jasoncohen/) shared one of the best
breakdowns I‚Äôve seen on how freemium works and how to reframe it to protect
your business. I‚Äôm sharing the most practical takeaways here, with additional
thoughts for operators trying to make this model work.

**Freemium isn‚Äôt a product strategy; it‚Äôs marketing spend**

Founders often treat freemium like it‚Äôs part of product development. ‚ÄúLet‚Äôs
give people access, see what they do, collect feedback, then improve.‚Äù

But here‚Äôs the catch: most of those users will never pay. Not even $1/month.

Their behaviour, feedback, and requests will skew your roadmap in the wrong
direction because they‚Äôre not your customer. They're tourists.

Instead, you should treat every free user like a marketing lead. Ask: How much
does it cost to support this user, and is the ROI worth it?

**Free users are not your ideal customers**

This is one of the most costly misunderstandings.

Free-tier users often look engaged they sign up, click around, and leave
feedback. But they rarely convert. Because the value of your product isn‚Äôt
high enough for them to pay. It‚Äôs not just a pricing issue it‚Äôs a fundamental
mismatch of need.

Real customers, the ones willing to pay $20/month or $200/month, have
different problems. You don‚Äôt hear their voice clearly in a freemium model
because they‚Äôre outnumbered 100:1.

And that feedback imbalance leads to misguided product decisions.

**Conversion rates are worse than you think**

Even legendary freemium products like Dropbox only saw [4% conversion (even
lower) ](https://www.toption.org/post/dropbox-monetization-strategy)from free
to paid. Most startups don‚Äôt come close.

In OpenView‚Äôs data, the average visitor ‚Üí freemium conversion was 6%. Freemium
‚Üí paid was 5%. Which means total visitor ‚Üí paid conversion was just 0.3%.

So if you‚Äôre paying for ads, SEO, or content, your actual CAC balloons. It
costs more to get paying customers through freemium than it does by selling
directly to the right audience.

**You still pay for tech support, freemium doesn‚Äôt exempt you**

Support is a hidden cost most founders forget. Free users ask questions. They
run into bugs. They expect answers.

You can route them to community forums or try ignoring support requests from
free users, but that erodes your brand and kills conversions. Or worse: it
makes your support team pick and choose who deserves help.

And if support is one of your competitive advantages, degrading it undermines
the entire product experience.

**Freemium has real upsides, but they‚Äôre marketing benefits**

There are real advantages to freemium, just not the ones founders usually
assume.

  * It lowers onboarding friction

  * It builds a pipeline for upsells

  * It boosts social proof ("10,000+ users")

  * It blocks competitors from winning the same user

But notice the pattern: these are all marketing outcomes. Not monetisation,
not retention, not product-market fit.

So if you want to use freemium, own what it is, a lead-gen channel, not a
growth strategy.

**So what‚Äôs the fix? Charge the marketing department**

If freemium is marketing, then account for it like marketing.

Estimate how much each free user costs: infra, support, operations. Multiply
by the number of users. That‚Äôs your real CAC under freemium.

Now compare it to your other channels: SEO, paid ads, partnerships. Which
gives you better ROI?

If freemium leads to lower CAC and better retention, great, invest more. But
if it‚Äôs dragging your business underwater while providing weak conversions,
cut the cord.

**The litmus test for freemium**

Ask yourself:

  * Would I pay this much per user if this were an ad campaign?

  * Am I tracking free-to-paid conversion like I track funnel performance elsewhere?

  * Do I know the total cost of running the free tier?

  * Am I treating this as a revenue-driving channel or just doing it because ‚Äúeveryone else does‚Äù?

If you‚Äôre not asking those questions, you‚Äôre not being honest about what
freemium costs you.

Remember, freemium isn‚Äôt evil. It works if you‚Äôre clear about what it‚Äôs for.
But if you think it‚Äôs a free way to learn, grow, and build, chances are, it‚Äôs
silently sabotaging your business. Reframe it.

And decide whether freemium is actually serving your startup, or just eating
it alive.

##### **üìÉ QUICK DIVES**

Almost every software product today is racing to add AI. But across all these
domains, one pattern keeps showing up: the AI implementations are eerily
similar. Most default to, ‚Äúlet‚Äôs just add a chat panel.‚Äù

And so, the same problems show up again and again.

[Luke Wroblewski](https://lukew.com/ff/entry.asp?2107) recently shared some
insights on these recurring issues. If you're building AI products or
integrating LLMs into existing apps, here are three patterns worth reflecting
on, with design ideas that can help.

  1. **Capability Awareness: What can this AI do?**

One of the biggest issues with AI chat interfaces is the ‚Äúinvisible interface‚Äù
problem.  
People don‚Äôt know what they can ask. And even when they do, they don‚Äôt know
how best to phrase it. Just like talented people, even capable AI has
strengths and weaknesses, but those aren‚Äôt always obvious.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!UBk2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-
post-
media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F99e9f72c-d649-491d-b9cf-f0641df22b43_1712x898.png)Image
Source: Luke Wroblewski

Without affordances or guideposts, users are lost.

**What helps:**

  * **Let AI rewrite the prompt.** Make the model auto-optimise people‚Äôs vague input into better prompts.

  * **Suggested Questions.** Highlight example prompts in the UI so users understand the boundaries and possibilities.

  2. **Context Awareness: What did the AI use to respond?**

If capability is about what AI can do, context is about how it did it.

Was it using a search? A user profile? Your chat history? External tools? Some
part of its training data?

With so many possible sources and context window limits, users don‚Äôt know
whether they can trust the output or if hallucinations are sneaking in.

**What helps:**

  * **Contextual Retrieval.** Add background info to prompts automatically, to reduce vagueness.

  * **Streaming Citations.** Show what articles, PDFs, or data the AI pulled from, in real time.

  * **Task-Specific Context Windows.** Use background agents for different tasks so they each stay focused.

  3. **Walls of Text: Why does everything feel like a giant scroll?**

AI chat has over-indexed on text. And while words are powerful, they‚Äôre not
always the best way to absorb information.

Most interfaces still spit out long paragraphs in a linear thread. People find
it hard to scan, extract, or recall what matters.

**What helps:**

  * **Structured Outputs.** Let users ask for slides, tables, spreadsheets, or visuals.

  * **Better UI for Long Responses.** Break responses into expandable sections or summaries.

  * **Inline Streaming Images.** Don‚Äôt just stream text, stream charts, images, or annotated screenshots too.

This is not a complete list, and we‚Äôre still early in the AI product cycle.
But these three themes, capability, context, and communication, are cropping
up everywhere.

So if you're building AI into your product, don‚Äôt just bolt on a chat window.
Step back and ask:

  * What should users know about what AI can do here?

  * How will they know where the answer came from?

  * And can we give them more than just a wall of text?

The bar is rising fast. Early builders who solve these problems elegantly will
shape the next generation of usable, trusted AI tools.

Most founders care deeply about the problem they‚Äôre solving. But here‚Äôs the
truth:

Investors don‚Äôt always know your problem exists.

That‚Äôs why one of your most important jobs in a pitch is to prove, not just
explain, that the problem is real.

You can‚Äôt just say:

> ‚ÄúMarketers struggle with this.‚Äù  
> ‚ÄúParents don‚Äôt want to carry car seats.‚Äù

That‚Äôs not enough. You need proof. You need to show them:

  * This problem is real

  * It affects a large group of people

  * Those people are looking for a solution

  * Your product is the answer

Let‚Äôs break this down.

Customer discovery is your secret weapon. It‚Äôs not about guessing what people
want, it‚Äôs about asking them.

Here‚Äôs the kind of proof that works with investors:

**Real conversations with real people.** Tell investors how many people you
spoke to. Be specific. For example:

> ‚ÄúWe talked to 1,000 families with kids under 5. 90% said they‚Äôd use this
> service weekly.‚Äù

**Clear data, not vague statements.**

Don‚Äôt say, ‚ÄúMost people liked it.‚Äù Say, ‚Äú87% said they would pay $10/month for
this.‚Äù

**Specific insights you‚Äôve learned.**

Maybe you discovered that grandparents are an overlooked group. Or that people
care more about trust than speed. These insights show you understand the
problem better than anyone else.

**Quotes from your users.**

Use 1‚Äì2 short quotes that capture the pain.

> ‚ÄúI avoid taking cabs because I don‚Äôt want to carry a car seat.‚Äù  
> Real words stick better than summaries.

**Patterns you noticed.**

Did you hear the same issue across different income levels? From people in
different cities? Investors love it when you show that this isn‚Äôt just your
circle, it‚Äôs a wide pain point.

You only get a few minutes to make your point. Here‚Äôs how to make it land:

**Start with the story, not the data.** Bring the problem to life first. Share
a quick, real example.

> ‚ÄúOne mom we spoke to said she avoids rideshares completely because of the
> car seat hassle. We heard this again and again.‚Äù

**Then drop the numbers.** Follow the story with the proof.

> ‚ÄúWe interviewed 1,000 parents. 90% said they‚Äôd use our solution weekly.‚Äù

**Keep it short and sharp.** Don‚Äôt list everything you learned. Just highlight
the 2‚Äì3 biggest takeaways and say you‚Äôve got more in the appendix or memo.

**Let the insight guide the product.** Connect what you learned to what you
built.

> ‚ÄúAfter dozens of conversations, we realized this wasn‚Äôt just about
> transportation, it was about trust and reliability. That‚Äôs why we focused on
> background-checked drivers with childcare experience.‚Äù

**Show you‚Äôre a missionary founder.** Don‚Äôt just dump data. Speak with care.
Show that you want to solve this, not just because it‚Äôs a big market, but
because it matters to you.

When you walk in with this kind of evidence, here‚Äôs what you‚Äôre telling
investors:

  * I‚Äôve done the work

  * I know who my users are

  * I‚Äôm building something people already want

  * I‚Äôm not guessing, I‚Äôve validated it

  * I‚Äôm committed to the long haul

That‚Äôs what separates a pitch deck from a real business.

You‚Äôre not just trying to prove your idea is smart. You‚Äôre showing that you
understand the problem inside and out. You‚Äôve listened. You‚Äôve learned. You‚Äôve
built something that makes sense, and you‚Äôve got the numbers and insight to
prove it.

That‚Äôs what gets investors to lean in.

‚ÄúYour North Star Metric is your strategy, and your strategy is your North Star
Metric.‚Äù Many first-time founders make mistakes in choosing the right metrics
for their startups. Even some don‚Äôt have a clear idea about it and have picked
the wrong North Star metrics. Remember, with your north star metrics,
investors predict how you are thinking about the future. So it‚Äôs important to
choose the right one.

It‚Äôs a single metric which can align the entire company around an overarching
mission whilst simultaneously reflecting the value being delivered to
customers.

Look at the mission statements of two of the world‚Äôs best-known companies,
Google and LinkedIn:

> Google ‚Äì ‚ÄúGoogle‚Äôs mission is to organise the world‚Äôs information and make
> it universally accessible and useful‚Äù
>
> LinkedIn ‚Äì ‚ÄúConnect the world‚Äôs professionals to make them more productive
> and successful.‚Äù

**The goal of the North Star Metric is to quantify this mission and express it
in a way that can be measured and tracked**. It is often used as a guiding
light for the entire company to rally around ‚Äì a sort of special key
performance indicator (KPI).

**What is a good North Star Metric?**

> Which metric, if it were to increase today would accelerate business
> flywheel? - Lenny Rachitsky

Here are some best practices for deciding on a good North Star metric:

  1. Is it measurable?

  2. It is simple, memorable and easily understood?

  3. Is it a leading (not lagging) indicator of success?

  4. Does it help your customers reach _their_ end goal?

  5. Does it apply to all customers, and does it add value to all customers?

**What is a bad North Star Metric?**

There are also some poor metrics to use as a north star, the most common of
which is revenue. Below are some characteristics you should aim to avoid when
deciding on a north star metric:

  * Lagging indicators that show past performance, not future potential.

  * Metrics are hard for employees to influence or understand their impact on.

  * Metrics do not reflect customer value delivery, like vanity metrics.

  * Generic metrics that don't represent your unique business strategy.

Your north star should be unique to your business (or at the least your
business niche) and should be an indicator of the problem you solve for
customers, and the value provided to them.

Below we have provided examples of North Star metrics from many well-known
brands. Notice how they are not all perfect, but generally, all reflect value
delivered to the market.

So while choosing metrics for your startup, remember - ‚ÄúNorth Star Metrics -
if this metric grows, all other metrics will be growing too. Such as
engagement, acquisition, and sales.‚Äù - Adam Wright.
