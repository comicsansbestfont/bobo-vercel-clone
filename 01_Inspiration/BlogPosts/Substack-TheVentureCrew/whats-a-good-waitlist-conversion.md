##### **üìú TODAY‚ÄôS DEEP DIVE**

Waitlists. They‚Äôre either a growth lever or a black hole where leads go to
die.

Every founder gets excited when a waitlist starts growing. A thousand signups.
Ten thousand. Feels like traction, right? But here‚Äôs the trap: Most waitlists
convert at just 2‚Äì3%. That‚Äôs not traction. That‚Äôs a leaky funnel with a
[vanity metric](https://theventurecrew.substack.com/i/159532998/how-to-spot-
vanity-metrics-in-startups-dont-fall-into-this-trap) on top.

In fact, many startups end up showcasing waitlist numbers as early ‚Äúproof‚Äù of
interest. But without real engagement or conversion, a waitlist is just a list
of emails ‚Äì not customers.

So what does a _good_ waitlist actually look like? And more importantly, how
do you build one that converts? [Scalemath](https://scalemath.com/) recently
shared a solid framework that founders can actually use, so I wanted to
highlight a few key points from it, along with some of my own thoughts:

**What‚Äôs considered a good waitlist conversion rate?**

The short answer: It depends.  
The better answer: here‚Äôs what benchmarks across industries show:

  * **If your conversion rate is 50% or more:** You‚Äôre doing better than most. Keep optimizing.

  * **If it‚Äôs under 20%,** Something‚Äôs off. Either your follow-up sucks, your product‚Äôs unclear, or both.

  * **If it‚Äôs under 5%:** Stop everything. You‚Äôve got a messaging, positioning, or audience mismatch.

**What actually drives waitlist conversion?**

A waitlist is only as good as what happens _after_ someone signs up. Let‚Äôs
look at what separates high-converting waitlists from dead ones.

1\. **Speed matters. The longer you make people wait, the colder they get.**

  * If users get access within a month, avg. conversion is ~50%.

  * If you keep them hanging for over 90 days, conversion drops below 20% - even worse. 

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LQiM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fea770875-7574-4e22-a9a7-ca5ad8b25b2a_3768x2512.png)Source:
Lennys newsletter

What to do: If you can‚Äôt onboard right away, have a clear engagement plan.
Keep them excited until they‚Äôre in.

**2\. Don‚Äôt ghost your waitlist. Talk to them. A lot.**

Most brands collect emails and go silent. Then, months later, they send one
‚ÄúWe‚Äôre live!‚Äù email and act surprised when nobody shows up.

What to do instead:

  * Send regular updates (progress, sneak peeks, behind-the-scenes).

  * Personalize it. Use names. Mention why they signed up.

  * Show waitlist position (‚ÄúYou‚Äôre #154 ‚Äì get early access by inviting 2 friends‚Äù).

> Example: Superhuman
>
> Superhuman didn‚Äôt just make you wait. They handpicked users, interviewed
> them, and gave each one a white-glove onboarding. It worked.

  3. **Use exclusivity, but don‚Äôt overdo it.**

People want what they can‚Äôt easily have. If everyone can join anytime, why
bother?

What to do:

  * Make the experience feel _earned_.

  * Highlight limited spots, invite-only access, or beta perks.

  * Show social proof (‚Äú5,000+ already signed up‚Äù).

> Example: Clubhouse  
> Clubhouse exploded because access felt like a secret club. People didn‚Äôt
> want to miss out ‚Äì and the waitlist created buzz that fed itself.

  4. **Give people something to** _**do**_**while they wait.**

Waiting should feel active. If people can move up the queue by sharing,
engaging, or referring, they will.

What to do:

  * Let users earn earlier access by inviting others.

  * Offer perks for engagement, not just signups.

  * Avoid over-relying on discounts ‚Äì they cheapen your brand.

> Example: Dropbox  
> Their referral-driven waitlist was legendary. Free storage for invites
> turned users into marketers ‚Äì and helped Dropbox scale to millions.

  5. **Segment your waitlist. Not all leads are equal.**

Some folks are just curious. Others are ready to pay. Treat them differently.

What to do:

  * Use data enrichment tools (like Clearbit) to learn more about your users.

  * Track engagement to spot high-intent leads.

  * Prioritize hot leads with early access or VIP treatment.

In the end, your waitlist is more than just a list. It‚Äôs your _first chance_
to build trust, spark interest, and convert early believers into loyal users.

Done well, it‚Äôs a growth engine that fuels momentum before you even launch.
Done poorly, it‚Äôs a graveyard of missed opportunity ‚Äî a long list of people
who _almost_ cared.

So don‚Äôt just collect emails. Nurture them. Segment them. Give them a reason
to stay excited. Give them a reason to share. And most importantly, give them
something worth waiting for.

Because a great product deserves a great first impression ‚Äî and your waitlist
is exactly that.

##### **üìÉ QUICK DIVES**

If you‚Äôre a B2B SaaS founder and not leveraging Reddit, you‚Äôre leaving leads
on the table.

I recently came across reddit marketing guide by [Adam
Shaw](https://www.linkedin.com/in/adam-shaw-stl/) so thought to share with you
some key points and my opinion with you. He shared - ‚ÄúReddit is the #2 source
of leads, right after LinkedIn. It‚Äôs where people are actively asking for help
and exploring new solutions‚Äîunlike LinkedIn, which is more about storytelling
and sharing success.

And here‚Äôs the kicker: 50% of my leads come from just answering questions on
Reddit for less than an hour a day.**‚Äù**

Here‚Äôs how you can do it

**Step 1: Turn Your Profile into a Landing Page**

Your Reddit profile is like a mini landing page. It‚Äôs the first place curious
users will go after reading your comments or posts. Make sure it‚Äôs optimized
to convert.

**How to set it up:**

  * Choose a username that‚Äôs tied to your brand or product.

  * Write an actionable profile name (e.g., ‚ÄúHelping SaaS Founders Scale‚Äù).

  * Add a CTA to your profile banner (e.g., ‚ÄúDownload our Free SaaS Marketing Guide‚Äù).

  * Include your one-line product pitch and CTA in the bio (e.g., ‚ÄúWe help SaaS founders grow faster. Subscribe to our newsletter below!‚Äù).

  * Add links to key resources like:

    * Lead magnets

    * Newsletter sign-ups

    * Product demo

**Step 2: Find Subreddits Where Your Audience Hangs Out**

Reddit is vast, but your audience is likely concentrated in a handful of niche
subreddits.

**How to find them:**

  1. Use Reddit‚Äôs search bar to look for your product keywords (e.g., ‚ÄúSaaS,‚Äù ‚Äústartup growth,‚Äù etc.).

  2. Use Google to search for ‚Äúyour keywords + Reddit‚Äù (pro tip: Google is better at finding Reddit threads than Reddit itself).

  3. Pick subreddits with active, engaged members in your niche.

**Pro tip:** Use tools like [Gummy
Search](https://gummysearch.com/?via=fdaae0) to automate finding relevant
subreddits and posts.

**Step 3: Prepare a Lead Magnet**

Redditors hate being sold to. They love **value-first** approaches.

That‚Äôs where a lead magnet comes in. It‚Äôs a way to engage your audience
without coming across as too salesy.'

**Examples of great lead magnets:**

  * In-depth guides tailored to specific problems.

  * Free trials of your product.

  * Newsletters with actionable advice (my favorite).

Make it easy for people to commit. For example, I use a newsletter landing
page to capture leads. By the time they get there, I‚Äôve already built trust
through helpful comments.

**Step 4: Start by Commenting, Not Posting**

Engage first, post later.

**Why?** Comments are more personal and allow you to connect directly with
people asking for help. You‚Äôll also learn what your audience needs.

**How to comment like a pro:**

  * Answer recent posts (24-48 hours old) for better visibility.

  * Avoid crowded posts (0-10 comments is the sweet spot).

  * Add value. Most comments are generic‚Äîstand out by being actionable, specific, and helpful.

**Step 5: Be the Best Commenter**

The secret to success on Reddit? Write better comments than everyone else.

**Here‚Äôs how:**

  * Be highly relevant‚Äîanswer only what‚Äôs asked.

  * Be specific‚Äîdon‚Äôt just say, ‚ÄúTry this.‚Äù Explain _how_ to try it.

  * Go in-depth‚Äîassume the person has zero knowledge.

  * Stay friendly‚Äîignore trolls.

**Make your comment stand out visually, too:**

  * Use white space for readability.

  * Add bullet points to organize your thoughts.

  * Be longer than most comments (Reddit loves thoughtful responses).

**Step 6: Post to Curate Conversations**

Once you‚Äôve spent a few weeks commenting, you‚Äôll notice patterns in what your
audience talks about. That‚Äôs when you start posting.

But don‚Äôt make the mistake of being self-serving. Redditors can smell that a
mile away.

**Here‚Äôs what works:**

  * Don‚Äôt post a blog or self-promotional content. Instead, ask questions that spark engagement.

  * Let the audience share their thoughts and experiences in the comments.

  * Use their responses to build conversations and authority naturally.

Reddit can be a goldmine for B2B SaaS founders if used the right way. Focus on
engagement first, provide value, and let the leads come to you.

Many founders believe that a great app idea is enough to make it successful.
But it‚Äôs not. What truly matters is the stickiness of your product.

Start thinking about your product in terms of where triggers, constraints, and
user rituals exist within it.

The rise of AI means that more user experiences within the best products will
be trigger-based, rather than static. And wide open experiences will be less
desirable than opinionated, constrained ones.

[I love this framework from Greg Isenberg](https://x.com/gregisenberg). It‚Äôs
the new strategy for thinking about how to keep users engaged with your
product.

  1. Trigger action with prompts

  2. Ignite FOMO with constraints

  3. Keep 'em coming back with rituals
