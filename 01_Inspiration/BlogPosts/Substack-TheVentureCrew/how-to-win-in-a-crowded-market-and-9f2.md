##### **üìú DEEP DIVE**

Here‚Äôs a well-known secret: VCs don‚Äôt love crowded spaces.

If a bunch of companies are chasing the same problem, it means brutal

Not exactly what investors dream of.

**But here‚Äôs the flip side‚Äîsome companies** _**do**_**win in crowded
markets**. And when the market is big enough, VCs will still invest. The key?
They need to believe they‚Äôre betting on the right startup.

So, if you‚Äôre a founder building in a competitive space, the real question is:
how do you stand out?

That‚Äôs exactly what we‚Äôre diving into today.

To make this real, let‚Äôs talk about a space that‚Äôs super crowded: AI.
(Agree??)

Specifically, AI-powered sales development representatives (SDRs)‚Äîtools that
help sales teams find and qualify leads faster.

(From my VC friend: We see at least one AI SDR startup every week. There are
plenty more out there raising money. So yeah, it‚Äôs competitive.)

That said, sales as an industry has a huge total addressable market (TAM), and
AI is, well, everywhere right now. VCs want to invest in this space. But only

**Which brings us to the challenge:**

  * How do you prove you won‚Äôt stall after some early traction?

  * How do you avoid insanely high customer acquisition costs?

  * How do you convince VCs you‚Äôre the one to bet on?

The answer: find a market wedge.

**The Market Wedge Strategy**

A wedge is simply a strategy to win a large market by initially capturing (1)
a tiny part of a larger market or (2) a large part of a small adjacent market.

So if you want to break into a crowded market, don‚Äôt try to be ‚Äújust another
AI sales tool.‚Äù Instead, specialize.

Example: Instead of being a generic AI SDR, you could be the AI SDR for
veterinarians.

**Why? Because focusing on a niche:**

  * Gives you a clear target audience with a unique need.

  * Makes customer acquisition _way_ easier.

  * Helps you stand out from the sea of generic competitors.

**But VCs won‚Äôt just ask, ‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs your wedge?‚Äù They‚Äôll ask, ‚ÄúHow do you grow
beyond it?‚Äù**

Here‚Äôs where your expansion strategy comes in:

  1. Vertical Expansion ‚Äì Offer more services to your niche.

     * You start with AI-powered sales for vets.

     * Then add appointment scheduling, inventory management, etc.

  2. Horizontal Expansion ‚Äì Expand to similar industries.

The key is to show investors that you‚Äôre thinking beyond today‚Äôs niche and
into tomorrow‚Äôs empire.

**Getting Customers (Without Burning Cash)**

A wedge is great. But without customers, it‚Äôs just a nice idea. VCs want to
see that you can acquire customers efficiently.

If you already have industry connections‚Äîamazing. Maybe you:

But what if you _don‚Äôt_ have these connections? That‚Äôs where partnerships can
be a game-changer.

For example, if you‚Äôre targeting veterinarians, you could:

  * Partner with some Medical Association to tap into their network.

  * Work with private equity firms that acquire vet clinics (and need them to grow).

  * Get backing from VCs specializing in pet tech, who can open doors.

> The goal is simple: show VCs you can acquire customers from day one‚Äîwithout
> breaking the bank.

Here‚Äôs another tip: **VCs love learning.**

If a founder teaches them something they didn‚Äôt know, it makes them _way_ more
likely to invest.

**That means your job is to be the expert.** You should know more about your
niche than anyone in the room.

How do you prove that?

  * **Challenge common assumptions:** _‚Äú_ Everyone thinks X about the veterinary industry, but actually, it‚Äôs Y.‚Äù

  * **Drop surprising insights:** _‚Äú_ Did you know 73% of pet owners would pay more for AI-enhanced vet services?‚Äù (Okay, I made that up, but you get the idea.)

  * **Offer a fresh perspective:** Explain why your approach makes more sense than the status quo.

And don‚Äôt shy away from the competition. **Yes, the market is crowded‚Äîbut
that‚Äôs because the opportunity is massive.** If you can prove you understand
the market better than anyone else, you position yourself as the founder VCs

So, if you‚Äôre in a crowded space and want to win investors over, here‚Äôs what
you need to do:

  * Find your market wedge‚Äîa niche that gives you an entry point.

  * Show how you‚Äôll expand beyond that wedge over time.

  * Prove you can acquire customers efficiently (without insane costs).

  * Know your market better than anyone else in the room.

In a crowded market, the loudest voices don‚Äôt win‚Äîthe smartest, most strategic
ones do.

So be different. Challenge assumptions. And make it impossible for investors

##### **üìÉ QUICK DIVES**

Even with $0 in sales, your startup can have a strong valuation if you
understand the levers investors pull. Valuation isn‚Äôt just about money coming
in; it‚Äôs about perceived potential, team credibility, and the size of the
opportunity.

**Pre-revenue reality:**

  * You don‚Äôt have revenue yet, but investors still see value in the story you‚Äôre building.

  * Early credibility builds trust with partners and makes it easier to attract top talent.

  * Market perception plays a big role; how you‚Äôre seen will influence deal terms and equity split.

**The common methods investors use:**

  * **Berkus Method** : Values five areas (idea, team, product, market, launch) at up to $500K each, capping most valuations at ~$2.5M.

  * **Scorecard Method** : Benchmarks your startup against similar ones, adjusting for strengths, weaknesses, and competitive landscape.

  * **VC Method** : Starts from a projected exit revenue, applies profit multiples, and works backwards to match investor ROI targets.

  * **Risk Factor Method** : Rates 12 risk categories from team quality to market size, adding or subtracting value based on each.

**Boosting your valuation before revenue:**

  * Build an MVP to prove you can deliver.

  * Showcase a strong, credible founding team with relevant track records.

  * Choose market comps that work in your favour.

  * Land early sales or pilots before fundraising to show momentum.

Recently, Greg shared an interesting [video on the IKEA
effect](https://x.com/gregisenberg/status/1955439410037821891) and how
founders can apply this in their startup:

People value things more when they‚Äôve built part of it themselves; that‚Äôs the
IKEA effect. Your customers can feel the same way about your product if you
design it right.

**How to make it work (with examples):**

Instead of everything pre-set, guide users to configure it themselves.

Example: Notion‚Äôs empty workspace prompts you to add your own pages and
templates you feel like you built your system from scratch.

Give customers building blocks they can arrange. Example: Canva lets you start
with a template but modify colours, text, and layouts turning ‚Äútheir‚Äù design
into ‚Äúyour‚Äù design.

Invite beta users to give feedback before launch. Example: Figma grew a
passionate community by running open betas and shipping features shaped
directly by user requests.

Offer flexibility rather than a one-size-fits-all solution. Example:
Airtable‚Äôs product looks simple at first, but users quickly mould it into CRM
systems, project trackers, or content calendars tailored to their workflow.

When users invest effort, they invest emotionally. That makes your product
harder to leave and easier to love.

Many startups proudly declare they‚Äôve found product‚Äìmarket fit (PMF). But
there‚Äôs a quieter, equally critical test: business-model‚Äìproduct fit (BMPF),
making sure the way you charge sustainably exceeds the cost to deliver.

Recently, [Chris Paik](https://x.com/cpaik) shared an interesting [article
titled as Cursor‚Äôs
problem](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1q3O7niwoxsyfJ5zSx8dgYzipEgBkUqXzLejQQ-
PQNWs/edit?tab=t.0). It shared - Cursor‚Äôs story is a cautionary tale.

They sold ‚Äúunlimited‚Äù coding on top of expensive variable-cost AI models from
OpenAI/Anthropic. That‚Äôs fixed revenue, rising costs, the same trap that
buried MoviePass, Oyster, and ‚ÄúUnlimited‚Äù ClassPass.

The danger signs:

  * **Cohort inversion** : light, profitable users churn; heavy, unprofitable ones stay.

  * **Topline masking rot** : growth hides deteriorating margins.

  * **Subsidies mistaken for product love** : usage spikes when prices are artificially low, but demand collapses when costs are passed on.

Uber and DoorDash survived early subsidies because they had a path to
operational efficiency (density, batching, utilisation) and future pricing
power. Without that bridge, subsidies just burn cash.

For variable-cost businesses, especially in AI, the lesson is simple:

  * Price in proportion to usage costs.

  * Cap or segment high-cost users early.

  * Test demand at the true marginal cost before declaring PMF.

> If customers disappear when the subsidy disappears, you don‚Äôt have PMF, you
> have **subsidy‚Äìmarket fit**.
