##### **üìú TODAY‚ÄôS DEEP DIVE**

[Matt Lerner,](https://x.com/matthlerner) former growth leader at PayPal and
founder of SYSTM, has helped hundreds of startups figure out one thing: **how
to actually grow.** And his answer is clear ‚Äî early on, founders need to lead
growth themselves.

It‚Äôs a necessary phase. Because when startups fail, it‚Äôs often not the market
or the product ‚Äî it‚Äôs that founders didn‚Äôt learn fast enough what really
drives traction. And no one can learn that for you.

Founders are deeply involved in product and sales early on ‚Äî but when it comes
to growth, many try to delegate too soon. Some get stuck in theory and
planning. Some keep building without validating. Others hand growth off to a
hire, expecting them to figure it out.

But the first growth hire can't do their job unless the founder has already
uncovered what works. You don‚Äôt bring in someone to scale chaos ‚Äî you bring
them in to scale signal.

Matt Lerner saw this mistake often as a VC. Founders would pitch go-to-market
strategies that looked impressive on a slide, but didn‚Äôt stand up to scrutiny.
There was no evidence behind them ‚Äî just ideas. And even if one of those ideas
worked, the upside was usually small.

> Lerner‚Äôs argument is simple:
>
> **Growth can‚Äôt be delegated until you know what works.** You don‚Äôt need
> polished strategy decks ‚Äî you need signal. That comes from firsthand
> experience.

At PayPal, Lerner saw that nearly all of their exponential growth could be
traced back to just a few key levers:

It wasn‚Äôt about doing everything. It was about doing the right things ‚Äî and
finding those things takes founder involvement.

That‚Äôs what pushed him to **define the concept of a ‚Äúgrowth lever‚Äù** ‚Äî a small
number of activities that drive most of the company‚Äôs growth. And finding
those levers is the founder‚Äôs responsibility.

Start by mapping your funnel from the moment a user hears about your product
to the moment they find value. You‚Äôre looking for drop-offs ‚Äî the stages where
people get stuck. You don‚Äôt need dashboards or analytics tools to do this. A
rough sketch is enough.

What matters is identifying the bottleneck that‚Äôs holding back your growth.
Then ask yourself: if this stage improved, how much would it impact the rest
of the funnel?

One example comes from Popsa, a photobook app that Lerner invested in. The
product was solid ‚Äî users liked it and stuck around. But install rates were
low. The team eventually realized the issue wasn‚Äôt the product ‚Äî it was the
App Store listing. The tagline ‚ÄúFast, Easy Photo Books‚Äù wasn‚Äôt specific
enough. People didn‚Äôt know what ‚Äúfast‚Äù meant. When they changed it to ‚ÄúPhoto
Books in 5 Minutes,‚Äù their conversion rate quadrupled.

Small change. Huge outcome. That‚Äôs the power of fixing the right bottleneck.

Behavioral data will tell you where people are dropping off. But it won‚Äôt tell
you why. That‚Äôs where qualitative research comes in.

Lerner leans heavily on the **Jobs to Be Done framework** here. Talk to your
actual customers ‚Äî not leads who didn‚Äôt convert, but people who did. Ask them
what they were trying to accomplish, why it mattered, and what their process
looked like.

You‚Äôll start to uncover patterns ‚Äî shared goals, repeated anxieties, common
alternatives they considered. This is what helps you position your product and
smoothen your customer journey.

Smart Tales, an educational app for kids, was struggling with conversions.
Customer interviews revealed something surprising: parents didn‚Äôt just want
their kids to learn ‚Äî they wanted to give them screen time without guilt. That
emotional insight shaped the company‚Äôs messaging and improved conversion rates
by 65%, without changing the product itself.

Once you know the bottleneck and understand your customer‚Äôs mindset, shift
into experimentation. This is where founders often get overwhelmed. There are
endless tactics and ideas. But Lerner simplifies it into weekly growth
sprints.

Each sprint is designed to answer one question: **does this change improve a
meaningful metric?**

The structure is simple:

  * Start with an observation and hypothesis

  * Make a team prediction

  * Run the experiment for a week or two

  * Review and decide what to try next

This isn‚Äôt about trying to get every test right. Most won‚Äôt move the needle.
But if you're running experiments tied to clear bottlenecks, you‚Äôll learn
something useful every time.

Take Sonic Jobs ‚Äî a job marketplace with high signup rates but low activation.
Their welcome email was too broad. After interviews showed that users looked
for specific job types, the team updated the email to include role-specific
links like ‚ÄúAmazon driver‚Äù and ‚ÄúWarehouse operative.‚Äù The result? Activation
doubled.

Sometimes you run a bunch of tests and nothing seems to work. That‚Äôs when you
zoom out and revisit customer outcomes.

Instead of focusing on why people didn‚Äôt convert, focus on why your best
customers _did_. What did they want? What did your product help them
accomplish?

If you offer a single clear outcome, you can lean into it. If your product
solves multiple problems, use onboarding questions to help customers self-
select ‚Äî just like Calm does. That makes it easier to tailor your messaging,
onboarding, and growth strategy to their specific goal.

Eventually, you‚Äôll spot the segment with the most urgent need and the highest
willingness to pay. That‚Äôs where you double down.

Founders often overlook great ideas because they feel too obvious or too
difficult. But many of the best growth levers aren‚Äôt new ‚Äî they‚Äôre just right
for your business.

Lerner gives the example of Fatmap, a mapping app with slow-loading content.
The team dismissed SEO as a channel because the maps took too long to load.
Lerner proposed a workaround: use static images while the map loads in the
background. That one shift increased traffic and later contributed to
acquisition.

Another trap is ‚Äúmental resistance‚Äù ‚Äî where you avoid a test because it feels
too simple to be meaningful. But if the test aligns with your bottleneck, it‚Äôs
worth running.

Even if you‚Äôre the one leading growth, you don‚Äôt have to do it alone. In fact,
your entire team should be plugged into growth.

Start by sharing your North Star Metric across the company. Then ask each team
member how their work impacts that metric ‚Äî and what they could do to improve
it. Bake that into their goals.

Lerner also encourages founders to make experiments public. Post them in
Slack. Let the team vote on variants. Show that everyone is learning together
‚Äî especially when things don‚Äôt work. That transparency builds confidence and
momentum.

The key shift here is cultural: growth isn‚Äôt something the ‚Äúmarketing team‚Äù
does. It‚Äôs something the whole company thinks about.

Eventually, you‚Äôll hit a point where you‚Äôre doing so many experiments that you
need help. That‚Äôs when it makes sense to build a growth team ‚Äî not before.

You don‚Äôt need a senior exec. Lerner recommends starting with:

  * A junior generalist who‚Äôs analytical, action-oriented, and curious

  * Or someone already on the team who‚Äôs shown interest in experiments ‚Äî like an analyst, product manager, or even support lead

The important thing is: hire once you know your levers, and you just need
someone to scale what you‚Äôve already figured out.

You wouldn't outsource your product before building version one. Don‚Äôt
outsource growth before you‚Äôve figured out how your product grows.

Founder-led growth forces you to talk to customers, isolate bottlenecks, and
test ideas that get results. It gives you insights into your product, your
users, and your positioning that no outside hire can match ‚Äî and once you‚Äôve
built that understanding, you can bring in others to scale it.

This isn‚Äôt a detour from building your company. It _is_ building your company.

reading](https://review.firstround.com/founder-led-growth-playbook).

##### **üìÉ QUICK DIVES**

I‚Äôve seen countless first-time founders confused about startup valuation. So I
am covering few question that I received multiple times here:

**What determines a company‚Äôs valuation?**

In its most basic sense, valuation can be calculated by multiplying the
outstanding shares by the price per share. The thing is that company valuation
is more of an art than a science due to the mix of qualitative and
quantitative factors that go into it.

Value of company = Shares outstanding * Price per Share

Below, I share some of the factors that Brad Feld mentions in Venture Deals,
followed by my opinion on each:

  1. Stage of the company:

Early-stage valuation is impacted by factors such as entrepreneur experience,
the perception of overall opportunity, and the amount being raised.

Later-stage valuation heavily relies on financial performance and projections.

  2. Funding competition: there is a positive correlation between the number of investors who want to fund a company and the valuation of such a company.

  3. Leadership team experience: because there‚Äôs a perceived negative correlation between more experienced entrepreneurs and risk, the valuations of companies with more experienced leadership teams will be higher. Note: personal biases will play a role here.

  4. Size and trendiness of the market: Your market size (e.g., TAM, SAM, SOM) and the growing demand of a company‚Äôs market will also have a positive correlation with valuation.

  5. An investor‚Äôs entry point: Some investors have a valuation investment range. For example, an investor might only invest in companies that are valued at $10M or less.

  6. Financials and other numbers: There is a direct correlation between how well a company‚Äôs financials and numbers stack up (against its given industry and competitor benchmarks) with the company‚Äôs valuation.

  7. Economic climate: There is a tentative positive correlation between the stock market‚Äôs performance and the valuation of a company. That‚Äôs to say that if the stock market is performing well, valuations will be higher and vice versa.

If many investors perceive a given company to be less of a risk and more of an
investment opportunity (aka shows signs of a successful exit) ‚Äî due to its
strong leadership, market size/trendiness, and numbers ‚Äî the valuation of a
company is likely to be higher than lower. It makes sense, no?

Remember, investors want to invest in the horse that‚Äôs most likely to win the
race.

**What are some methods that investors use to value companies?**

Investors may calculate valuation by assessing:

  * Comparables: the valuation of other companies with a similar profile

  * Multipliers: industry revenue/EBITDA/EBIT multipliers

  * Formulas, such as pre-money valuation = ($1M x #Engineers) ‚Äî ($1M x #MBAs) AND Net Present Value (discounted cash flows)

  * Venture Capital/Leveraged Buyout Method

  * Monte Carlo Simulations

You will find more info about all these and another method later.

The most common question that I received from every founder,

**Is it mandatory to calculate a company‚Äôs value to fundraise?**

Valuation in fundraising has several benefits. At a basic level, founders will
know exactly how much of the company they‚Äôve sold and they‚Äôll also have a
cleaner cap table. Valuation will also make life easier for everyone involved
‚Äî investors and founders.

That said, companies don‚Äôt need to value their companies in order to
fundraise, at the start. There are multiple fundraising vehicles that allow
for a postponed valuation to take place in a future and larger fundraising.
Such mentioned vehicles include, but are not limited to, the:

Convertible Note ‚Äî Investors give companies money in exchange for an
opportunity to buy the stock at a discount in a future financing round. Until
then, the ‚Äúinvestment‚Äù is considered debt that pays interest. If you want to
know more about it, check out our previous article.

SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) by Y Combinator. A SAFE is neither
debt nor equity and has no maturity date or accruing interest. You can also
check about this in our home page.

**Should companies aim for the highest valuation they should get?**

No. Because a too-good-to-be-true kind of deal is likely to hurt everyone
involved in the next round. If you have a high valuation in one round, then
fail to hit the right milestones and end up having to raise at a lower
valuation, that‚Äôs going to dilute your original shareholders. If that happens,
it‚Äôs possible that they will block a new financing. No founder wants that ‚Äî
it‚Äôs already hard to run a company and fundraise.

Also, if a company raises at higher valuations, the company‚Äôs investors will
expect a bigger exit and will block a sale if they are not happy with the
selling price.

I hope this helps you to get an idea of what‚Äôs an important thing to know
during valuing your company.

Starting a business with co-founders is like embarking on a long road trip
together. While the journey can be more enjoyable with partners who complement
your skills, even the strongest relationships need ground rules. This is where
a founder agreement comes in.

Simply put, a founders agreement is a legal contract between company founders
that sets clear guidelines for their business relationship. Think of it as a
roadmap that outlines everyone's rights, roles, responsibilities, and
expectations from day one.

**Why You Need One**

While not legally required, having a founder agreement is crucial because it:

  * Prevents misunderstandings by getting everyone on the same page

  * Provides a clear structure for handling disputes

  * Shows potential investors you're serious and well-organized

  * Protects both your business and personal relationships

As [Simon Bacher, CEO of Ling](https://www.linkedin.com/in/simonbacher/), puts
it: "My wife and I co-founded our startup, and we saw a founders agreement as
essential - think of it like a prenup for your business."

**1\. Basic Company Information**

**2\. Roles and Responsibilities**

  * Each founder's specific duties and areas of authority

  * Clear job titles and their limitations

  * Flexibility for role changes as the company grows

**3\. Decision-Making Powers**

**4\. Equity and Vesting**

  * Each founder's ownership percentage

  * Vesting schedule (typically four years with a one-year cliff)

  * Conditions for receiving full rights to shares

  * Compensation plans

**5\. Commitments and Resources**

  * Expected time commitment from each founder

  * Initial capital contributions

  * Intellectual property rights

  * Other resources (industry connections, expertise)

**6\. Exit Planning**

  * Conditions for dissolving the company

  * What happens during an IPO

  * Handling of unvested shares

  * Non-compete and non-solicitation terms

Now that you know what goes into this document, here are a few founder‚Äôs
agreement templates and examples to help you craft your own.

  * [University of Pennsylvania Law](https://www.law.upenn.edu/clinic/entrepreneurship/startupkit/founders-agreement.pdf): Includes provisions about ownership structure, transfer of ownership, decision-making and dispute resolutions, representations and warranties, and choice of law

  * [Harvard Business School](https://startupguide.hbs.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/Founders-Agreement-Template.docx): Includes provisions on ownership, competition restriction, and exit situations

You‚Äôre going to go through many ups and downs with your partners. Creating a
founders agreement is an excellent way to ensure you‚Äôre all protected
throughout your business journey.
