If you‚Äôre building a SaaS startup, it‚Äôs easy to obsess over top-line ARR
targets and forget the mechanics that actually drive those numbers. The truth
is: you can only improve what you measure. And your sales funnel gives you the
clearest picture of where revenue is being created ‚Äî or leaking.

Here are the four funnel metrics you should track (with practical ways to
apply them):

  1. **Lead volume & Lead Velocity Rate (LVR)**

     * Track how many qualified leads you‚Äôre getting every month.

     * Calculate LVR = (This month‚Äôs qualified leads √∑ Last month‚Äôs qualified leads) ‚Äì 1.

     * Example: You had 200 qualified leads in June and 250 in July. LVR = (250 √∑ 200) ‚Äì 1 = 25% growth.

     * Pro tip: Set your LVR ~15% higher than your revenue growth target. If revenue is growing 10% MoM, your leads should grow ~25% MoM.

  2. **Conversion rates across funnel stages** \- Don‚Äôt just measure ‚Äúleads to won deals.‚Äù Break it down:

     * Lead ‚Üí Opportunity = (Opportunities √∑ Leads) * 100

     * Opportunity ‚Üí Won = (Won deals √∑ Opportunities) * 100  
Why it matters: If lead‚Üíopportunity is strong but opp‚Üíwon is weak, the problem
isn‚Äôt marketing ‚Äî it‚Äôs sales performance, pricing, or product fit.

     * Example: Out of 500 leads, 100 became opportunities (20% lead‚Üíopportunity). From those, 25 closed (25% opp‚Üíwon). Overall conversion = 5%.

  3. **Sales velocity** \- This metric tells you how fast you‚Äôre turning pipeline into revenue:

     * Formula: (Leads √ó Deal value √ó Conversion rate) √∑ Sales cycle length

     * To speed velocity: increase lead flow, push up average deal size, improve conversion %, or shorten your sales cycle.

     * Example: 100 leads √ó $5,000 avg deal √ó 10% conversion √∑ 60 days = $833/day in velocity. 

     * If you shorten the sales cycle to 45 days, velocity jumps to $1,111/day ‚Äî without adding leads.

  4. **CAC & CAC payback by channel** \- Not all leads are equal. You need to calculate:

     * CAC = (Acquisition costs + Sales + Onboarding costs) √∑ New customers

     * CAC Payback = CAC √∑ Monthly contribution margin

     * Example: Channel 1 CPL = $100. You need 70 leads to land 1 customer = $7,000 CAC.  
Channel 2 CPL = $200. But only 20 leads needed for 1 customer = $4,000 CAC.

     * If your gross margin is $500/month per customer:

       * Channel 1 payback = $7,000 √∑ 500 = 14 months

       * Channel 2 payback = $4,000 √∑ 500 = 8 months

     * Higher CPL doesn‚Äôt always mean worse economics ‚Äî look at funnel conversion + payback.

Audit your funnel this month. Pick one lever (conversion, cycle, or channel
mix) to optimise, instead of blindly pushing for more top-of-funnel leads.
Often, the fastest way to hit ARR targets is by fixing funnel efficiency, not
by just ‚Äúgetting more leads. (Read detailed writeup
[here](https://www.airtree.vc/open-source-vc/sales-funnel-metrics-you-should-
be-tracking-in-your-saas-startup))

The ‚Äúyoung and fast IPO‚Äù startup story is fading. According to University of
Florida data, the median age of a company at IPO was 13.5 years in 2024, far
older than the 5‚Äì9 year median from the 1980s‚Äì2000s.

Why are startups older at exit?

  * Private capital is abundant. With trillions flowing into private markets, companies don‚Äôt need to rush to public markets for funding.

  * Public company burden. Compliance, reporting, and scrutiny make IPOs less attractive.

  * Push for profitability. Markets expect sustainable models before rewarding IPOs.

What this means for investors:

  * Longer holding periods. Be prepared to wait years longer for liquidity.

  * Shift to later-stage. Growth and pre-IPO deals may gain more weight in portfolios.

  * Rise of secondary sales. More investors may seek liquidity through secondaries, though these aren‚Äôt always easy or guaranteed.

How to adapt:

  * Extend fund lifecycles beyond 10 years

  * Diversify across early and late stages

  * Build secondary sales into your strategy

  * Prioritise startups focused on capital efficiency and sustainable growth

The quick win here: If you‚Äôre investing in startups, recalibrate your
expectations. The path to exit is slower, but with the right balance across
stages and effective liquidity planning, you can ride this trend instead of
being caught off guard by it. (Read the full breakdown
[here](https://microventures.com/time-to-exit-are-startups-getting-older) )

The frothy pandemic-era valuations are finally correcting. In Q2, 25% of VC
rounds were flat or down, the highest share in a decade. Nearly every big IPO
priced well below its last private round.

But the story isn‚Äôt uniform:

  * AI is the outlier. Median Series B step-up for AI startups is 2.1x, versus 1.4x for non-AI. Faster cycles, bigger checks, and resilient multiples define the sector.

  * Policy-linked sectors (AI, defence, fintech, crypto) are holding stronger, partly because of government tailwinds (e.g., Circle doubled on debut after progress on the GENIUS Act).

  * Normalisation is healthy. It frees startups from inflated markups that made new rounds or exits unrealistic, even if uncertainty still clouds public markets.

Don‚Äôt view the reset as a collapse; see it as a clearing. Strong startups now
raise on cleaner terms, and AI‚Äôs premium shows where capital conviction
remains strongest. ([Read full report
here](https://pitchbook.com/news/reports/q2-2025-us-vc-valuations-and-returns-
report))

One of the most common mistakes founders make after raising their first round
is copy-pasting their pitch deck into their website. But here‚Äôs the truth:
investors and customers care about very different things.

  * Investors want the big picture ‚Äî vision, TAM, defensibility, team strength.

  * Customers want to know one thing: ‚ÄúCan this product solve my problem today?‚Äù

If your homepage still sounds like your seed pitch, you‚Äôll lose customers who
just want clarity and confidence, not a roadmap of your 5-year vision.

Here‚Äôs a simple framework ([from Emily
Kramer](https://review.firstround.com/how-to-adapt-your-pitch-deck-into-your-
website/?)) to translate your pitch into customer-facing messaging:

  1. **Problem slide ‚Üí Homepage hero**  
Don‚Äôt say: ‚ÄúRadiologist-read images are frequently misinterpreted.‚Äù  
Do say: ‚ÄúReduce diagnostic errors.‚Äù  
Customers want today‚Äôs pain described in their language.

  2. **Solution slide ‚Üí Plain description of what your product does now**  
Don‚Äôt say: ‚ÄúCompliance and security management platform.‚Äù  
Do say: ‚ÄúSOC 2 audit platform.‚Äù  
Clarity beats cleverness every time.

  3. **Market size slide ‚Üí Targeted ICP callout**  
Don‚Äôt say: ‚ÄúWe‚Äôre targeting remote-first and hybrid companies globally.‚Äù  
Do say: ‚ÄúBuilt for early-stage remote startup teams.‚Äù  
The more specific, the faster customers say, ‚ÄúThis is for me.‚Äù

  4. **Vision/Why now slide ‚Üí About page, not homepage**  
Your website‚Äôs job isn‚Äôt to inspire belief in your billion-dollar market ‚Äî
it‚Äôs to show why you‚Äôre useful today. Save the big vision for your story page
or founder‚Äôs note.

The quick win here? Audit your homepage. For every line of copy, ask: Is this
written for a VC or a customer? If it‚Äôs VC-speak, rewrite it in your
customer‚Äôs words.

Your website doesn‚Äôt need to sell the future of your category. It needs to
sell the next signup.

building without relying on more VC.

Why it‚Äôs gaining traction:

  * The ZIRP era of unlimited capital is over‚Äîfundraising discipline is back.

  * GenAI is giving startups a huge efficiency boost, making profitability realistic earlier.

  * There was never a label for this ‚Äúone-and-done‚Äù approach, until now.

Reactions are mixed. VCs like Eric Bahn and Elizabeth Yin see it as a smart
path for software startups, while others argue ambition often requires more
capital.

Founders, on the other hand, are fans, Josh Payne (StackCommerce) says, it
gave him investor validation and networks without heavy dilution.

The bigger insight: most companies don‚Äôt become unicorns. There are 34.8M
small businesses in the US, but only 30,000 hit $100M+ in revenue.

For many internet startups, one round is enough to find product-market fit,
get the default alive, and build on your own terms.

Founders ask me all the time what to say in a pitch. But here‚Äôs the better
question: What are you asking them?

If you‚Äôre giving up equity, make them earn it. The power dynamic is broken if
you‚Äôre doing all the prep and they‚Äôre just judging. Previously, we shared a
write-up on questions founders should ask VCs in a fundraising meeting.

But I recently came across a post by [Joseph (GP at Daring
Venture)](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/activity-7325192898784694272-Bg-D/),
where he mentioned three questions every founder should ask a potential
investor:

  1. **What does "hands-on" actually mean to you?**  
Everyone claims to be value-added. But does that mean weekly check-ins,
midnight strategy calls, or just quarterly board meetings where they tell you
what‚Äôs wrong? VCs define support very differently‚Äîknow what you‚Äôre signing up
for.

  2. **When was the last time you backed someone who didn‚Äôt fit your pattern?**  
This reveals how they think. If their ‚Äúexample‚Äù is funding someone from
Georgia Tech instead of Stanford‚Ä¶ that‚Äôs just surface-level. Great VCs know
that breakthrough ideas don‚Äôt come from cookie-cutter backgrounds.

  3. **What‚Äôs the hardest piece of feedback you‚Äôve given a founder?**  
The founder‚Äìinvestor relationship will hit rough patches. You want someone who
can tell you hard truths with empathy‚Äînot someone who avoids conflict or
sugarcoats reality.

The best founders don‚Äôt just pitch. They interview. Because capital is
everywhere, but the right investor relationship is rare.

Most founders still treat an MVP as a hacky prototype just to prove an idea.
That doesn‚Äôt work in today‚Äôs crowded markets.

The better play is: build a competitive wedge by scoping _down_ your users
instead of scoping _up_ your product.

Here‚Äôs how you can apply it this week:

  * Pick your smallest possible audience. Instead of ‚Äúfounders‚Äù or ‚Äúteams,‚Äù choose a hyper-specific profile. For example, Linear didn‚Äôt target ‚Äúall developers‚Äù ‚Äî they started with individual contributors at small startups who hated issue tracking.

  * List their top 3 frustrations. Don‚Äôt guess. Ask them, run a short survey, or even use your waitlist form to capture pain points. Only build for the frustrations you hear repeatedly.

  * Ship for that audience only. Make it clear that your early version is not for everyone. Invite only those who fit your profile, so you avoid churn and gather sharper feedback.

  * Use your waitlist as a filter. Don‚Äôt just collect emails ‚Äî ask 3‚Äì5 qualifying questions (role, company size, current tool, their biggest pet peeve). This way, you can handpick the right testers instead of getting noisy, unfocused feedback.

The win: Instead of trying to ‚Äúvalidate‚Äù an idea in theory, you create a
**usable, competitive product for a narrow slice of the market** ‚Äî and that
slice becomes your proof of product-market fit. ([Read detailed
here](https://linear.app/now/rethinking-the-startup-mvp-building-a-
competitive-product))
