##### **üìú DEEP DIVE**

Sales at an early-stage startup often feel like wandering in the dark with a
flashlight that barely works. You hear a lot of vague advice: ‚ÄúJust hustle,‚Äù
or ‚ÄúGo get ‚Äòem!‚Äù But when you‚Äôre the founder or first salesperson trying to
land those critical first customers, what you need are tactics, not
motivational quotes.

I came across an interesting post shared by [Whitney
Sales](https://www.linkedin.com/in/whitneysales/), founder of ThoughtForge,
creator of The Sales Method, and one of the sharpest sales minds. She shared
three powerful sales frameworks that I believe every founder should know and
use.

So I‚Äôm sharing some key points, along with my thoughts on them:

**Why?** Because at this stage, you are the first customer.

According to Whitney, ‚ÄúThe inception of any company is inevitably linked to
the challenge the founder first faced and addressed. This part of the
narrative is too often forgotten, and it‚Äôs key to connecting with a customer.‚Äù

Before you‚Äôve got fancy logos and metrics, you‚Äôve got your pain and your
reason for building the product. Use it.

**The Exercise:** Use this simple story-building template to craft a value-
based founder narrative:

> ‚Äú[SUBJECT] [ONCE UPON A TIME], [SITUATION] [CUSTOMER PROBLEM]. [CUSTOMER]
> and realized [FEATURES OF PROBLEM]. [COST]. [SUBJECT] learned [IDEATION
> PROCESS]. As a result, [SOLUTION].‚Äù

This isn‚Äôt fluff. It‚Äôs how you relate, build trust, and show the human reason
behind your product.

Example: The TalentIQ founder started his first company and lost time and
money because a recruiter couldn‚Äôt send qualified candidates fast enough. He
saw the chaos of 50 tabs open across scattered data sources, and realised
recruiters lacked one clean source of truth. That pain led to the product.

You‚Äôve already told this story to yourself, your early hires, and maybe
investors. Tell it again, but now to your customers.

**Why?** Because the founder‚Äôs story won‚Äôt always resonate with every buyer.

Whitney says, ‚ÄúIf you only have one customer, extract the elements of their
use case that are most relatable to your prospect. A beta customer‚Äôs story can
be as valuable as a paid customer‚Äôs story.‚Äù

This is where your product‚Äôs _real-world_ value starts to come through. It
builds credibility and defuses objections.

**‚Üí The Exercise:** Here‚Äôs Whitney‚Äôs customer story framework:

> ‚ÄúOne of my clients, [CUSTOMER NAME], who is in the same [QUALIFICATION
> CRITERIA], was having the same problem. When I met with their [TITLE], they
> mentioned that [CUSTOMER PAIN POINT]. [DETAIL]. We implemented [FEATURE],
> and enabled them to [BENEFIT]. They saw [RESULT].‚Äù

üìå Example: A client had onboarding emails that weren‚Äôt working users dropped
off. Whitney‚Äôs team implemented behaviour-based emails that matched user
activity. The result? 63% more conversions. $4.2M in revenue unlocked.

Even if you have just one beta user, document their story. Get specific. Then
build a bank of these stories, segmented by company size, pain point,
industry, or tech stack.

**Why?** Because stories make people open up.

Early sales aren‚Äôt about pitching features. It‚Äôs about using founder and
customer stories to get a prospect to start talking. Whitney emphasises: ‚ÄúThe
best sign a pitch is working? The customer starts asking you questions.‚Äù

**The Structure:** Here‚Äôs her proven sales conversation flow:

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!RKYj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-

Source: First Round

  1. **Start human (5‚Äì10 min):** Crack a joke. Be real. Show you‚Äôre not a robot.

  2. **Assert the agenda (2 min):** ‚ÄúI‚Äôd love to understand how you do ___ today, then share how we might help.‚Äù

  3. **Ask value-based questions (5‚Äì10 min):** Learn their pain points. What‚Äôs costing them time or money?

  4. **Tell a customer story (2‚Äì3 min):** Match their pain to a real use case. Share what worked.

  5. **Go deeper into ROI (5‚Äì10 min):** Tie their pain to your product‚Äôs benefits. Use their own words to frame the solution.

  6. **Decide or qualify (10+ min):** Ask about their budget, timeline, and who else needs to be involved (a.k.a. the classic BANT framework).

  7. **Lock next steps (5 min):** Don‚Äôt end the call on ‚Äúwe‚Äôll be in touch.‚Äù Schedule the next meeting now.

If you‚Äôre an early-stage founder, your sales pitch is not your deck. Your real
pitch is your **founding story** and your **first customer win**.

Before you have a product that sells itself, you need stories that _speak_ for
it.

Whitney puts it best:

> ‚ÄúThe first sale founders make with these stories is with themselves. Then
> they use them to hire, raise money, and attract beta users. Scale happens
> when these stories are passed on to your salespeople.‚Äù

If you‚Äôre starting from zero, these three frameworks will get you your first
few wins. And those first few wins? They‚Äôre what unlock everything else.

I highly recommend reading [this article.](https://review.firstround.com/the-
three-frameworks-you-need-to-kick-start-sales/)

##### **üìÉ QUICK DIVES**

Founders love the idea of a waitlist. It feels like growth. The optics are
great, exclusivity, buzz, FOMO, and a line of people waiting to get in. But as
Gaurav Vohra, former head of growth at Superhuman, explains, most waitlists
don‚Äôt drive growth at all. They quietly kill momentum.

In his essay ‚Äú[The Waitlist
Delusion,‚Äù](https://substack.gauravvohra.com/p/the-waitlist-delusion) he
breaks down what founders get wrong about waitlists, when they actually make
sense, and why they usually backfire.

He learned this firsthand running Superhuman‚Äôs massive 500K+ waitlist, which,
while great for early buzz, ultimately hurt conversion and slowed growth over
time.

Here‚Äôs what every founder should take away:

**When a waitlist actually makes sense**

The only valid reason for a waitlist is when you have a _supply constraint_
that prevents you from serving new users properly.

  * **Hard product constraint:** You physically can‚Äôt serve more customers (e.g. hardware units, limited compute, unsupported devices).

  * **Soft product constraint:** Your product isn‚Äôt ready for all use cases or quality standards yet.

  * **Service constraint:** You lack enough people to onboard or support customers.

If you don‚Äôt have one of these three, you don‚Äôt need a waitlist. Everything
else ‚Äî hype, optics, ‚Äúcommunity building‚Äù ‚Äî is noise.

**Why waitlists hurt more than they help**

  * **You lose conversions fast.** At Superhuman, live signups converted to paid users at around 10%, while waitlisted users converted at just 3%. The longer someone stayed on the waitlist, the worse it got ‚Äî conversion halved every additional year they waited.

  * **You lose momentum.** Startups run on speed. A waitlist pauses your growth engine and delays critical learning loops ‚Äî from user feedback to product-market validation.

  * **You waste focus.** Teams end up managing ‚Äúthe queue‚Äù ‚Äî writing nurture emails, sending updates, running referral gimmicks ‚Äî instead of building the product that actually fixes the supply problem.

**The common traps founders fall into**

  * ‚ÄúIt builds hype.‚Äù ‚Äî Maybe for a few months, then it fades. If the product is great, real word-of-mouth will do far more.

  * ‚ÄúIt enhances our brand.‚Äù ‚Äî Works for luxury fashion, not software. People want access, not anticipation.

  * ‚ÄúIt nurtures prospects.‚Äù ‚Äî You‚Äôre better off giving high-intent users access and educating the rest separately.

  * ‚ÄúIt looks good for investors.‚Äù ‚Äî Only as a short-term signal. It‚Äôs not a repeatable growth motion.

**What good waitlists actually look like**

  * **OpenAI (DALL¬∑E 2):** Clear communication about why the waitlist existed, when it would end, and what users could expect. They closed it in five months ‚Äî perfect execution.

  * **Superpower (2024):** Used a waitlist only because of genuine logistical limits (blood tests and clinician access), then killed it quickly after scaling operations.

  * **Superhuman:** Initially justified, but kept it too long ‚Äî a lesson in how even a successful waitlist becomes dead weight once constraints are gone.

**What founders should do instead?**

Use a waitlist only as a short-term bridge ‚Äî not a branding exercise. Make
fixing the supply constraint your top priority. The moment you can handle
demand, kill the waitlist.

Because growth doesn‚Äôt come from a longer line at the door, it comes from
letting people in.

Founders often ask: how much should I raise, what do I need to prove, and how
do I actually close a great seed round? Here‚Äôs a practical playbook you can
use:

  1. **Figure out how much to raise**

     1. Model for a 24‚Äì36 month runway. Median time from seed to Series A is ~23 months (Carta data).

     2. Add a 25% buffer. Unexpected things always happen.

     3. Example: If your burn is $60k/month ‚Üí $1.8M for 30 months ‚Üí round up to ~$2.25M with buffer.

     4. [Simple spreadsheet ](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1LIU-gXocWcKD5pCNXzFhL5TmKkbt3eUsFD7nA21AIS4/edit?gid=0#gid=0)\- how does this model work out?

  2. **Prove the basics before you pitch**

     1. Proof of commitment: quit your job, show you‚Äôre all in.

     2. Proof of work: talk to 30+ SMB buyers or 100+ consumers. Listen for 40%+ saying ‚Äúwow, when can I get this?‚Äù

     3. Proof of insight: either a working prototype with early users or a clear memo/deck that explains your unique thesis.

  3. **Run a compressed fundraising process**

     1. Block 2‚Äì3 weeks on your calendar. Speak to as many investors as possible in that window.

  1.      1. The compression creates FOMO and scarcity, which pushes decisions.

  2. **Do your homework on investors**

     1. Build a target list with check size, sectors, whether they lead, and reputation with other founders.

  1.      1. Use Crunchbase, LinkedIn, and backchannel references to prep. Or you can download the [investors‚Äô contact database](https://theventurecrew.gumroad.com/) here.

  2. **Polish your materials**

     1. Keep it minimal: a clear deck or memo, a budget, and a[ simple model](https://theventurecrew.gumroad.com/l/financialmodeltemplate).

     2. Make it visually sharp ‚Äî good design = signal of quality.

     3. Recommended templates: [Sequoia](https://slideshare.net/slideshow/sequoia-capital-pitchdecktemplate/46231251?utm_source=chatgpt.com), [YC](https://ycombinator.com/library/2u-how-to-build-your-seed-round-pitch-deck?utm_source=chatgpt.com), [40 deck](https://theventurecrew.gumroad.com/l/startuppitchdecks?layout=profile)

  3. **Line up strong intros**

     1. Best: portfolio founders of the VC, respected operators, or other active investors.

     2. Avoid: intros from lawyers/service providers or investors not investing in your round.

     3. If you don‚Äôt have an intro, send a crisp cold email ‚Äî low hit rate, but worth trying.

  4. **Practice your pitch**

     1. Write a memo, even if you don‚Äôt share it. It [sharpens your story.](https://theventurecrew.gumroad.com/l/pitchstorytellingtemplate?layout=profile)

     2. Rehearse with ‚ÄúB-list‚Äù investors first. Iterate before you hit your top targets.

     3. Expect investor questions to carry more weight than your slides. Prepare FAQs.

  5. **Use angels to build momentum**

     1. Open a [SAFE](https://theventurecrew.gumroad.com/l/startuplegaldocuments?layout=profile) and let smaller checks come in early.

     2. This creates social proof that makes bigger VCs move faster.

  6. **Manage the close carefully**

     1. Don‚Äôt reveal names of other investors ‚Äî just describe in general terms.

     2. A round isn‚Äôt closed until funds are wired. Push for quick close dates.

     3. Treat verbal commitments as meaningless until you see a signed SAFE/term sheet.

If you take away one thing: seed rounds are less about a perfect business and
more about running a tight, well-planned process that creates momentum and
scarcity.
