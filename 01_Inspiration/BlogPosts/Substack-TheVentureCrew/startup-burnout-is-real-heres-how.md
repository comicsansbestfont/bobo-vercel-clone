  * The launch underperforms

##### **üìÉ QUICK DIVES**

We‚Äôre living through a historic technology shift with AI.

But here‚Äôs the catch: while product-building has changed dramatically in the
last two years, distribution hasn‚Äôt kept up.

[Brian Balfour](https://substack.com/@brianbalfour) recently published one of
the most important essays I‚Äôve read, and I couldn‚Äôt stop thinking about it. So
I‚Äôm summarising key ideas here, plus adding a few takeaways and warnings for
founders, builders, and operators.

We‚Äôre building AI-powered products in 2025 using the same playbooks from 2015.

SEO traffic is collapsing. Social platforms are walled gardens. Discovery is
dying.

The old growth channels (Google, Facebook, LinkedIn) are no longer reliable.
And the new ones haven‚Äôt fully emerged. That‚Äôs the dangerous gap we‚Äôre sitting
in.

> ‚ÄúAI is actively destroying the distribution channels we‚Äôve relied on for
> decades.‚Äù

Every major platform follows the same 3-step cycle:

  1. **Identify the moat** : What makes the platform defensible? (For Facebook, it was the social graph. For Google, it was search data. For Apple, it was the app ecosystem.)

  2. **Open the gates** : Make it easy for developers/creators to build on top. Offer viral mechanics, generous APIs, and monetisation splits.

  3. **Close and monetise** : Once escape velocity is reached, the platform tightens control. Growth tools disappear. Fees rise. Organic reach vanishes.

This isn‚Äôt theory. It‚Äôs a repeating, predictable pattern:

  * Apple let anyone build on the App Store, then absorbed top apps and enforced a 30% cut

  * Google gave free SEO traffic, then filled SERPs with ads and scraped answers

If you're building on a platform and not preparing for this cycle, you're
walking straight into a trap.

Brian believes the next distribution platform will be ChatGPT (OpenAI) and
that we‚Äôre already in Step 2: Opening the gates.

Here‚Äôs why:

  * ChatGPT has 700M+ users and is showing elite retention

  * OpenAI is actively launching connectors (HubSpot, Box, GitHub)

  * They're hiring a dedicated Agent Platform team

  * They‚Äôre laying the foundation to become the new interface, not just an app, but an OS layer for work

> Their moat? Context + memory.

Not just answering prompts, but remembering who you are, what you‚Äôve done,
what you want next.

Once you feed your data, workflows, and habits into their ecosystem‚Ä¶ switching
becomes extremely hard.

> Past platform shifts played out slowly:

With AI? The timeline might be even shorter. Developers are already racing to
integrate. Users are building muscle memory. The window to get early
advantages is now.

If you‚Äôre late, you‚Äôll still have to integrate, but you‚Äôll miss the upside and
enter just in time for the platform taxes.

  1. **Integrate early, but not blindly**  
Ride the wave to acquire users, get visibility, and stay competitive. But
don‚Äôt hand over your entire value chain.

  2. **Build a moat inside your product**  
Own your data. Build community. Create differentiation that‚Äôs not tied to
OpenAI‚Äôs API.

  3. **Assume the gate will close**  
Have a plan for when distribution gets taxed, APIs get limited, or ChatGPT
launches a feature that replaces your app.

  4. **Diversify your channels**  
Use the distribution while it lasts, but don't depend on it. Keep building
your brand, email list, or independent acquisition loops.

> ‚ÄúUse ChatGPT‚Äôs distribution, but don‚Äôt let ChatGPT become your product.‚Äù

If you‚Äôre building SaaS, AI tools, or content products, this shift affects you
now, whether you realise it or not.

The winners of the AI era won‚Äôt just build with better tech. They‚Äôll see the
distribution shift coming and position themselves before the herd arrives.

Every platform looks friendly in the beginning. That‚Äôs by design.

The smartest founders? They partner with the platform, profit during the
‚Äúopen‚Äù phase, and prepare for the ‚Äúclosed‚Äù one.

Don‚Äôt be caught off guard. The next great distribution shift isn‚Äôt coming.
It‚Äôs here.

A cap table, or capitalisation table, is a chart typically used by startups to
show ownership stakes in the business. It lists your company's securities
(i.e., stock, options, warrants, etc.), how much investors paid for them, and
each investor's percentage of ownership in the company.

You can obtain a stockholder ledger from your lawyer, which lists all
stockholders and shows how many shares or options they hold. However, I don't
consider this document a comprehensive cap table. In this, I am sharing a cap
table template that is used by various leading VC firms‚Äô partners.

> **[Click here to download the Excel
> sheet.](https://theventurecrew.gumroad.com/l/captabletemplate)**

The basic outlines of this cap table are:

  1. It shows all the major stockholders of the company listed on the left side. It also shows the major option holders and buckets of option holders

  2. It shows all of the classes of stock and how much was paid for them across the top of the columns

  3. For each investor, you show how much of each class was bought and how many shares of that class they own as a result

  4. You total up the cost and shares and then calculate ownerships on a fully diluted basis (which means you include the options, whether issued or non-issued or vested or non-vested).

I like this presentation for its simplicity and because it shows the
progression of financing activity. It also has the benefit of showing how much
each investor has put in on a cost basis, which many cap tables leave out.

If you want to make a cap table for your company, feel free to replicate this
format. If you have angel investors, put them in the angel section. I would
include the largest ones and bucket all the rest into ‚Äúother angels.‚Äù
