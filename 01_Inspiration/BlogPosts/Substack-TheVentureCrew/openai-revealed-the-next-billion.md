##### **START WITH**

Many founders assume adding AI coding assistants (like Cursor, Copilot, Claude
Code) will 10x developer output. The reality is more complicated.

Here‚Äôs what [research and real teams
](https://www.cerbos.dev/blog/productivity-paradox-of-ai-coding-assistants)are
finding:

  1. **Speed vs. perception**

     * METR‚Äôs July 2025 trial: developers with AI were actually 19% slower on OSS tasks, but they felt 20% faster.

     * The dopamine loop of instant code makes it feel productive, even when review/debug cycles erase the gains.

  2. **The quality trap**

     * 66% of devs in the 2025 Stack Overflow survey said AI outputs are ‚Äúalmost right but not quite.‚Äù

     * More context = more irrelevant noise. Senior engineers often spend more time fixing AI code than writing it from scratch.

  3. **Security & compliance risks**

     * Apiiro‚Äôs 2024 study: AI-generated code had 322% more privilege escalation paths and 153% more design flaws.

     * Secrets exposure rose 40%, often due to hard-coded credentials. For SOC2/GDPR/HIPAA companies, this is a real compliance red flag.

  4. **The 70% problem**

     * AI gets you to a demo fast (70%), but the last 30%‚Äîedge cases, tests, production readiness‚Äîis still human-intensive.

     * For juniors, AI scaffolding feels magical. For seniors, it often slows them down.

  5. **Business vs. dev reality**

     * Leaders love the ‚Äú10x‚Äù pitch, but real bottlenecks are design reviews, QA, system dependencies‚Äînot typing speed.

     * Expect incremental wins in boilerplate and onboarding, not exponential leaps.

**Why this matters for founders**

  * Don‚Äôt over-index on AI as a productivity silver bullet.

  * Use AI assistants for prototypes, docs lookup, or junior onboarding.

  * For core product and security-sensitive code, invest in solid engineering practices first.

AI coding assistants are accelerators for demos and MVPs, but not replacements
for senior engineering or rigorous review. Treat them as scaffolding, not
shortcuts.

If you‚Äôre raising funds in 2025, prepare for the slowest, toughest fundraising
cycle in recent memory.

  * **Time-to-close keeps climbing** : PitchBook‚Äôs Q2 2025 report shows the average private capital fund now takes 19.7 months to close, up from 18.9 months in 2024 and 14.6 months in 2019. Managers are being told to expect to still be in the market a year after first close.

  * **LPs are dictating terms** : Zero management fees, guaranteed co-investment rights, board seats, and LPAC representation are all on the table. Instead of rushing into first close, LPs now wait for the final close ‚Äî even paying late interest fees ‚Äî so they can see half the portfolio before writing a check.

  * **Liquidity crunch is fueling caution** : With exits delayed, distributions are drying up, leaving LPs with less fresh capital to reallocate. GPs are resorting to continuation vehicles or portfolio company loans to buy time until market conditions improve.

The pendulum has swung firmly to LPs. Transparency, creative structuring, and
patience are mandatory ‚Äî the old playbook of quick closes and standard 2/20
economics won‚Äôt cut it in this market. ([Read the full report here by
Pitchbook](https://pitchbook.com/news/reports/q2-2025-global-private-market-
fundraising-report))

PitchBook [data](https://pitchbook.com/news/articles/why-serial-founders-
still-have-a-fundraising-edge-over-first-timers) shows repeat founders have a
clear fundraising edge ‚Äî and the gap is only widening.

  * **Valuation premium** : Startups led by serial entrepreneurs are valued 2‚Äì3x higher than those led by first-timers, across every stage. The gap starts early and compounds as companies scale.

  * **Deal size advantage** : VCs are writing bigger checks to repeat founders, even if their prior companies didn‚Äôt have blockbuster exits. Investors call it a ‚Äúflight to safety‚Äù ‚Äî track record matters more than novelty.

  * **Macro pressures amplify bias** : Uncertainty around trade policy, rising AI build costs, and a still-tight liquidity environment push investors toward founders they see as ‚Äúknown quantities.‚Äù Serial entrepreneurs fit that bill.

**Why this matters:**

  * For **first-time founders** , the bar is higher than ever ‚Äî investors want proof you can deploy capital efficiently. The upside? You‚Äôre less constrained by playbooks and can take bolder bets.

  * For **investors** , the ‚Äúflight to safety‚Äù might backfire. As Chris Neumann warns: chasing serial founders reduces variance but also reduces potential fund outperformance.

In today‚Äôs market, fundraising is tilted toward repeat founders. First-timers
will need sharper narratives, early traction, or unique technical edges to
stand out.

Most founders think of product-market fit as a binary ‚Äúyou have it or you
don‚Äôt.‚Äù But in vertical SaaS, PMF is more like a spectrum ‚Äî you move through
urgency, repeatability, and scalability in layers.

Here‚Äôs a stage-by-stage playbook based on the Healthy Vertical SaaS PMF by
Stage framework ([Shared by Euclid
Venture](https://insights.euclid.vc/p/nobody-wants-your-product)):

**Level 1 ‚Äì Pre-Revenue (Inception / Pre-Seed)**

  * What matters: Finding urgent demand, not just ‚Äúlogical demand.‚Äù

  * Practical move: Talk to design partners and confirm the hair-on-fire problem in your ICP. Don‚Äôt sell a product ‚Äî uncover a job-to-be-done.

**Level 2 ‚Äì First Customers (Pre-Seed / Seed)**

  * What matters: Urgency validated, early customers willing to pay.

  * Practical move: Focus on 2‚Äì3 early adopters who are standard-bearers in your vertical. Don‚Äôt chase every use case ‚Äî double down where urgency is highest.

**Level 3 ‚Äì Signs of Traction (Seed)**

  * What matters: Repeatability ‚Äî early customer wins that look similar.

  * Practical move: Founder-led sales should now shift into a crude but working GTM. Hire an ‚Äúindustry evangelist‚Äù who speaks your customer‚Äôs language and can open doors.

**Level 4 ‚Äì Early Growth (Seed / Series A)**

  * What matters: High retention + referrals = proof of pull.

  * Practical move: Codify the sales motion. Start exploring expansion products or segments but only after you‚Äôve nailed your ICP. The first adjacent product should prove you can be more than a feature.

**Level 5 ‚Äì Scaling (Series A+)**

  * What matters: Scalability ‚Äî customers spread you via word-of-mouth, CAC drops, ARPU grows.

  * Practical move: Leverage early success into building a platform. Layer in new products, customer segments, and distribution models that widen your moat.

Most vertical SaaS failures don‚Äôt die because the product was bad. They die
because founders confused early validation with repeatability, or mistook
logical ROI for urgent demand. This framework helps avoid that trap by
matching PMF levels with the right founder focus at each funding stage.

Every founder has felt it: that sting of watching a hype-fueled startup with
no product and no revenue raise millions, while your cash-flow-positive,
growing business struggles to even get a meeting.

It feels unfair. And it is.

But there‚Äôs a deeper logic behind it. One that has little to do with how
‚Äúgood‚Äù your business is and everything to do with how venture capital works.

Recently, Steve Blank broke [down](https://steveblank.com/2025/07/01/why-
investors-dont-care-about-your-business/) why so many real businesses with
customers, revenue, and even profits often fail to attract VC attention. The
core idea? Venture capital isn‚Äôt built to fund good businesses. It‚Äôs built to
fund massive ones.

> Most founders misunderstand what investors are actually looking for. They
> assume that solid traction and profitability should be enough.

But venture capital follows a different playbook entirely.

Here‚Äôs what many founders miss:

  * **VCs are in the business of power-law outcomes.** They need 10x‚Äì100x returns to make [their math](https://theventurecrew.substack.com/i/155318590/steps-to-know-if-your-company-can-return-a-vc-fund) work. A steady-growing $20M company might be a great business but it‚Äôs not the billion-dollar rocket ship they‚Äôre hunting for.

  * **Your startup is a financial instrument.** The moment you raise venture money, your company becomes part of a portfolio. Investors aren't just betting on your growth they‚Äôre betting on a future exit (IPO, acquisition, or secondary sale) within 7-10 years.

  * **Perception drives value.** Especially now, when[ IPOs are rare and secondaries are a popular](https://www.cardumencapital.com/blog-posts/secondaries-reshaping-the-exit-landscape-for-vc-funds) liquidity path, what matters is how compelling your company looks to the next investor in line. The story you‚Äôre telling has to be big, bold, and believable.

This is also why certain sectors become hot overnight. Once a few top-tier
firms back AI companies, everyone wants in. The result?

  * Floods of capital chase a handful of ‚Äúhot‚Äù categories

  * Even questionable ideas get funded if they have the right narrative

  * Meanwhile, proven but ‚Äúunsexy‚Äù companies get ignored

> And secondaries have quietly changed the game. With fewer public exits, many
> investors are making money by selling their stakes to other funds. These
> aren‚Äôt new shares, they‚Äôre early investors cashing out while the company is
> still private.
>
> But this only works if the startup‚Äôs perceived value keeps rising so hype
> and optics matter more than ever.

So what can you do?

  * **Understand the game.** Your business may be amazing, but that doesn‚Äôt mean it‚Äôs venture-scalable. And that‚Äôs okay.

  * **Match your story to the investor.** Some funds chase trends. Others back overlooked gems. Know which one you‚Äôre pitching.

  * **Don‚Äôt assume they‚Äôll ‚Äúget it.‚Äù** You have to show them why your company can be massive and do it in a way that aligns with their timelines, incentives, and risk appetite.

Because once you take VC money, their model becomes your reality. If you don‚Äôt
fit what they‚Äôre looking for or don‚Äôt know how to frame your business in a way
that does you‚Äôll keep hearing ‚Äúno‚Äù for reasons that have nothing to do with
the actual quality of what you‚Äôre building.

And if you do have the kind of story they‚Äôre chasing? Now‚Äôs the time to go
big. Just remember: what they want isn‚Äôt a business. It‚Äôs a breakout.

Every time a new platform emerges, we scramble to measure its impact. But
early on, it‚Äôs rarely clear what to measure or why it matters.

We saw this with the early internet. In the 90s, people tracked things like
‚Äúinternet hosts‚Äù or ‚Äúhits‚Äù, which just counted how many files your browser
downloaded from a web server. More GIFs meant more hits. Not exactly
insightful.

Then came the smartphone wave, and the confusion shifted to installed base vs.
unit sales vs. ARPU.

Social media added its evolution: first it was ‚Äúregistered users,‚Äù then MAU,
then DAU/MAU ratios. Each wave had its metrics, often chosen to make a company
look good, not to reflect actual value.

We‚Äôre at that same messy phase with generative AI.

Benedict Evans recently [broke this down](https://www.ben-
evans.com/benedictevans/2025/6/9/generative-ais-metrics-question) brilliantly.
He pointed out that many of today‚Äôs AI metrics sound impressive but don‚Äôt tell
us much.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SwLQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-
post-
media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F536f158e-deb3-4b58-b4fe-9fcf0ee3c5d0_960x540.bin)Image
Source: Google‚Äôs blog

Even when the numbers are specific, they often lack clarity:

  * If a model becomes more efficient, the same task needs fewer tokens, so are we seeing a drop in usage or just better technology?

  * On the flip side, agents and media tools burn more tokens per request, so growth might reflect heavier workloads, not more users.

And we‚Äôre still guessing at what generative AI will become. That makes
choosing the ‚Äúright‚Äù metric nearly impossible.

> Will it mostly remain as user-facing chatbots? Or will it quietly integrate
> into everything, like SQL or cloud compute?

If it becomes infrastructure used everywhere but rarely seen, then asking ‚Äúhow
many people use AI?‚Äù is as pointless as asking ‚Äúhow often do you use a
database?‚Äù

So what should we be tracking?

  * **Engagement quality** ‚Äì not just logins, but how deep and meaningful the interaction is.

  * **Retention over time** ‚Äì are people coming back, and are they using it more meaningfully?

  * **Task replacement** ‚Äì what jobs or workflows are users abandoning in favor of AI tools?

  * **Behavioural feedback** ‚Äì are people satisfied on the first try, or do they constantly?

  * **Enterprise integration depth** ‚Äì is it casual use by the marketing team or core to ops?

Other things like how companies like Google and Meta measure deeper signals:
reformulated searches, bounce rates, completion behaviour. These are second
and third-order indicators that show what‚Äôs working, and they often feed back
into making the product better.

Right now, generative AI lacks those feedback loops. If you ask an LLM a
question and don‚Äôt try again, was the answer perfect? Or did you give up and
switch to Google? No one can tell.

At the end of the day, all metrics eventually resolve to money and time.
That‚Äôs where clarity will come from, only once we know what generative AI is
truly for. Until then, we‚Äôre still counting hits. (You can read the original
[article here](https://www.ben-evans.com/benedictevans/2025/6/9/generative-
ais-metrics-question).)
