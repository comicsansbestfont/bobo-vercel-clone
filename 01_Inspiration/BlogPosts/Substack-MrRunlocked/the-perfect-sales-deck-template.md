A sales deck is a presentation that you and your sales team use to pitch your
product to potential customers (unlike a pitch deck that‚Äôs being used to pitch
to investors).

Most of the time the sales deck is being used during [product sales
demos](https://startupbusinesstips.substack.com/p/the-ultimate-guide-to-sales-
product-demos) \- but (a slightly modified version) can also be used for
prospecting and demo follow-ups (material that‚Äôs being provided to buyers
after the demo via email or uploaded to digital sales rooms like
[Simpliciter](https://simpliciter.io/)).

It‚Äôs a core sales material, being used throughout the buyer journey:

  1. Prospecting (e.g. Outreaching with the [TAPSA framework](https://startupbusinesstips.substack.com/p/cold-email-template))

  2. (Virtual and in-person) Product Demos (‚Äî> [Ultimate Sales Demo Guide](https://startupbusinesstips.substack.com/p/the-ultimate-guide-to-sales-product-demos))

  3. Follow-Up material

  4. or even for pre-recorded demos, webinars, or onboarding sessions (‚Äî> especially for [PLG companies](https://startupbusinesstips.substack.com/p/product-led-vs-sales-led-saas-growth))

Your sales deck lays the foundation for good storytelling and helps to get
your [messaging and positioning](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/the-ultimate-
saas-messaging-framework) right.

A great sales deck:

**Slide 1: Cover Slide**

Add your company name, company logo, and your 1-sentence description.

> ü§î  _'Sounds cool, the design looks also good...Let's get started...'_

**Slide 2: Market Insight / Macro trend**

Set the context. Share your insight about the market. What's changing and why
it matters?

> ü§î  _'Wow. That's shocking/interesting/awesome. Curious how/why this is
> relevant...'_

**Slide 3: The problem**

What is the problem for them? Why it is a real pain?

> ü§î  _'Oh shit. I have this pain and it sucks...'_

**Slide 4: Status Quo (Old way)**

Describe the (shitty) status quo of your ideal customers.

> ü§î  _'That's me. I know this situation...'_

**Slide 5: Solution (New way)**

High-Level Information about your Product

> ü§î  _'I get the context of WHAT you do. I'm interested in more details.'_

**Slide 6: Solution**

Highlighting the top 3 Capabilities & Features

> ü§î ‚Äò _I now understand what I can do with the product (but not how - it's not
> a training)'_

**Slide 7: Product Demo (‚Äî > Now you switch to screen sharing your product)**

Now you switch to presenting your product (the actual 'product demo')

> ü§î  _'Focus on the main USECASES and how it solves the problem of the
> prospect'_

**Slide 8: Benefits**

Explain what's in for them! Focus on max. 3-4 benefits.

> ü§î  _'That's cool (and believable and relevant).I want the same results.'_

**Slide 9: Trust/Social Proof**

Add Social Proof elements. Case Studies, Testimonials, Reviews, Badges, Logos.

> ü§î  _'Wow, they work with very similar companies than us. They need to be
> good'_

**Slide 10: Pricing**

How do you charge clients? What pricing plan is for whom?

> ü§î  _'Okay, I understand that plan X is right for us & it costs X‚Ç¨ + I see a
> realistic ROI'_

**Slide 11: FAQs/Objections**

Proactively share the most common objections and your answers to them (concern
handling).

> ü§î  _'Yes those were actually my concerns. But this sounds solid. They earn
> trust points.'_

**Slide 12: Next Steps / CTA**

What do you want them to do next? Provide guidance. (Free trial, Book demo,
etc.)

> ü§î  _'I exactly know what I need to do next. Will do this right now...'_

So now you know the secret structure of a powerful sales deck. 12 slides,
clear structure, powerful story.

> Stop procrastinating with equity grants promised to your team.

It‚Äôs a common mistake I see way too often: founders making equity promises to
new hires and postponing the actual grant process several months, sometimes
years, later.

There are 3 main reasons why you should avoid procrastinating with equity
compensation:

  1. **The value of your equity and your cap table will change when you don‚Äôt expect it and you‚Äôll have to retro-engineer everything**

     1. If you communicate equity granted in $-value or in % of ownership (instead of a fixed number of shares/options) you might struggle to reconcile promises made to team members at different points in time

     2. We‚Äôve seen clients at Easop who raised several rounds of financing (via SAFE notes with different caps or via priced rounds) and communicated a $-value to be granted to team members as they joined the company (which is a great way to make it tangible)

     3. If you communicate a % of ownership, be sure to know what was the fully diluted count at the moment of the offer otherwise you‚Äôll have to retrace the full history of the company‚Äôs cap table

  2. **The exercise price cannot be defined based on the date of the promise, it will be based on the date of the actual grant so the more you wait, the less favorable it will be for your team members**

     1. The strike price or exercise price of options is generally based on the fair market value of the company at the moment of the grant, not at the moment of the promise

     2. You can generally make a vesting start at the moment of the promise but the exercise price will remain the same for the full batch of grants you make even if team members have joined earlier than others and would have benefited from a lower exercise price if their grants had been made when they joined.

  3. **If an employee leaves the company it is impossible to make a grant a posteriori**

     1. In general, one of the rules that the stock option plans state is that equity can only be granted to team members who are active employees or service providers

     2. If you postpone equity grants for too long and have people who, in theory, actually deserve an equity grant that has been promised but leave the company before it‚Äôs implemented, the company will struggle to honor its promise
