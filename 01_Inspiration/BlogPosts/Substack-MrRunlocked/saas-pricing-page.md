The goal isn‚Äôt to create the flashiest pricing page. It‚Äôs to create one that

The Hero section of the pricing page is what you see at the very top when you
visit. It‚Äôs important to remember that the hero section on a pricing page
isn‚Äôt the [hero section on your
homepage](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/i/146545088/step-transforming-messaging-

The pricing page is typically more of a down-funnel visit. Meaning it is
primarily for someone closer to the consideration/purchasing stage, rather
than a first-time visitor who is just learning about your company.

Because of this, your pricing page does not need to be created for a first-
time visitor.

‚ùå You don‚Äôt need to give them a ton of information up at the top of the page.  
‚ùå You don‚Äôt need to tell them what your product does.

They probably already know that, and have a lot of context around it. Any
generic copy related to your company is unnecessary.

So instead, use the section to explain one or more of:

> 1Ô∏è‚É£ How can they start (for free) using your product
>
> 2Ô∏è‚É£ Why different types of customers might want different plans
>
> 3Ô∏è‚É£ How the product scales with them (being used by different types/sizes of
> companies)
>
> 4Ô∏è‚É£ What ‚Äòmore‚Äô they get if they upgrade to a higher tier
>
> 5Ô∏è‚É£ Emphasizing USP in your pricing (for red ocean markets) ‚Üí See livestorm
> example

That would make good value-add copy on your pricing page.

A good example here is Linear.

They don‚Äôt do anything fancy with the header, just Pricing.

The Pricing Menu is the most important section of a pricing page.

The goal of the Pricing Menu should be to communicate truly essential
information to visitors and to give them as much context as possible to
determine how they can work with you.

We see a lot of companies overload the Pricing Menu with exhaustive details,
and feel strongly that‚Äôs a mistake. One of the counterintuitive things about
pricing pages is that

**‚ÄúThe tighter your pricing menu, the more it looks as if you have really
thought through how to differentiate your plans.‚Äù**

‚úÖ The goal of the pricing menu should be to show people the key levers and the
key differentiators between plans that might determine which one they‚Äôre a fit
for.

‚ùå The goal should not be to provide an exhaustive feature list that shows
every single differentiator and forces visitors to compare a bunch of numbers.

They can get that in the feature table. If you‚Äôre going to create a feature
comparison table anyway, then including all that information at the top is
redundant.

From the pricing menu, a visitor should be able to understand 6 core elements:

**1Ô∏è‚É£ Pricing Modality**

‚Üí How you charge.

Options include Flat Fees (e.g. 50‚Ç¨/month), Licenses (e.g. 50‚Ç¨/month/user),
Usage-Based (e.g. 0,1‚Ç¨ per contact), Credits (e.g. 100‚Ç¨ for 1000 credits), or
a combination.

**2Ô∏è‚É£ Pricing Metrics (aka. value metric)**

‚Üí The unit(s) you charge for (e.g., Users, GPUs, APIs, etc).

P.S. Learn how to [choose the right value
metric](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/i/134485840/step-choose-your-value-metric)
for your SaaS.

**3Ô∏è‚É£ Value Ladder (aka. feature gating)**

‚Üí How value scales (e.g., collaboration, automation, governance, etc).

Usually, there‚Äôs a feature ‚Äútheme‚Äù that scales the value of a product. Popular
options are collaboration, automation, or sophistication. Make that clear in
the pricing menu.

**4Ô∏è‚É£ Packaging**

‚Üí How you organize your modality, metrics, and features into bundles, usually
as discrete tiers. Although some companies don‚Äôt do this, they opt for ‚Äòpay as
you go‚Äô or √† la carte packaging.

**5Ô∏è‚É£ Price**

‚Üí How much each option costs, and the billing period.

**6Ô∏è‚É£ Call to Action**

‚Üí What visitors should do next. Usually, either get started, free trial,
request a demo, or contact sales.

The concepts above probably sound more complicated than they actually are.
Ultimately, in the pricing menu, you want to help a visitor quickly understand
how your pricing strategy works and how much it will cost them.

The goal is to help them understand both those things in less than a minute,
preferably 30 seconds. If you can do this without doing anything fancy, by all
means do it ‚Äì the simpler the better.

Going back to Linear, they communicate all of this extremely efficiently.

And also, Calendly does a pretty good job.

Within a few seconds, visitors understand:

Here‚Äôs a list of recommendations to organize your pricing menu‚Äôs core
elements:

**1Ô∏è‚É£ Recommendation 1: Keep your Plan Name simple**

People love to obsess over plan names. We don‚Äôt think this makes a difference.
Keep it straightforward. But this is just something that people are used to
seeing first, so start there. Common terminology you see a lot is:

  1. Free

  2. Basic

  3. Starter

  4. Standard

  5. Pro

  6. Premium

  7. Plus

  8. Teams

  9. Business

  10. Enterprise

**2Ô∏è‚É£ Recommendation 2: Put the monthly cost of your price**

Under the plan name, you want to put the monthly cost (also for the yearly
plans) and make sure that you‚Äôre pinning that monthly cost to whatever your
pricing metric is.

**3Ô∏è‚É£ Recommendation 3: Include the billing period(s)**

You want to make sure that with the price, you‚Äôre including the billing period
and how that works. A common way to do this is a toggle up above the pricing
grid.

**4Ô∏è‚É£ Recommendation 4: Highlight Key Features and Usage Thresholds**

Once you have the plan name, the price, and the billing period, you want to
make sure that for each plan, you are highlighting the core features and usage
thresholds that come with each plan. The key here is to keep it concise. Try
to stick to 5 variables, only more if really needed. Include only the usage
metrics that are really important to drive price, and any features that are
critical for plan differentiation.

**5Ô∏è‚É£ Recommendation 5: Add specific CTAs**

The only other thing that is an absolute must is a call-to-action. You want to
have a button for each tier guiding what a visitor should do next.

If it‚Äôs a free plan or you lean more PLG, it‚Äôs likely ‚ÄúGet Started.‚Äù  
If you lean more sales-led, it could be ‚ÄúRequest a Demo‚Äù or ‚ÄúContact Sales.‚Äù

Importantly, each plan can have a different CTA depending on how it fits into
your GTM motion.

Those are really the core elements of a pricing menu. Beyond that, everything
else is nice to have. If you can communicate everything you need to across
those variables, do it. You don‚Äôt want to make your pricing page more
complicated than it has to be.

**#6 Enterprise Tier**

The FAQ section should act as your final line of defense before somebody gets
in touch with sales. The idea is that 99% of a visitor‚Äôs questions can be
answered by the time they get through the FAQ section.

If there‚Äôs anything left over that needs to be answered, that‚Äôs what the FAQ

  1. **Make it accordion-style/expandable.** Otherwise, it will look like a wall of text.

  2. **Limit it to 8-12 questions max.** If you have more than that, you can probably be clearer in the sections above.

  3. **Link to full documentation.** Provide links to your help center, knowledge base, or support section if necessary.

The sections above are the most important pieces of a pricing page. However,
there are many optional sections to consider. Breaking those down below:

**# 1 Social Proof**

Social proof can take many forms, including a logo wall, customer quotes,
testimonials, stats, or G2 or Capterra ratings.

Companies drop social proof all over the place, the 3 most common sections
being underneath the Hero section, between the Pricing Menu and the Feature
Comparison Table, and between the Feature Comparison Table and the FAQ.

We don‚Äôt have a hard opinion about it, but in the interest of communicating
essential information as quickly and clearly as possible, we would recommend
moving the social proof under the Pricing Menu (and not in the Hero).

Adding relevant logos can be especially powerful for strengthening your
positioning. For instance, if you‚Äôre positioning your product as a solution
for innovative companies, and OpenAI is a customer, that‚Äôs great social proof.

Another good one is Mixpanel, which shows customers by tier:

**#2 Add-ons and Professional Services**

There are two ways to approach add-ons and professional services:

  1. Provide all information on the pricing page.

  2. List the Add-Ons and Professional Services and link to another page.

Intercom does a great job of adding its add-ons to the pricing page.

Another example is Vidyard, who add its add-on directly on the pricing table.

**#3 Pricing Calculator**

We discussed the slider earlier, but a pricing calculator can be helpful if
you have multiple products, multi-year contract terms, and other variables
that influence the final price point. HubSpot is a great example: they link to
the pricing calculator directly from the pricing page.

**#4 Discounted Plans (Startups, NGOs)**

If you sell to startups and nonprofits, you might offer a dedicated discounted
plan for them.

Asana offers a 50% discount for NGOs.

**#5 Chat Widget**

If you decide to have a live chat embedded on your website, adding it to your
pricing page makes sense, especially to ensure questions are answered and to
escalate questions to a live support person.

Just make sure that if the chat widget is answering questions, it can do so
reliably and accurately, and if you‚Äôre routing to another person, they are
consistently available on short notice.

**#6 Trust Signals Footer**

This includes security badges, payment icons, support availability, and
migration assistance if applicable. There‚Äôs likely redundancy with the FAQ
section. Include these, especially if, like security badges, they‚Äôre a deal-
breaker for prospective customers.

Miro has a ‚Äòsecurity and compliance‚Äô section on their pricing page.

Building an effective pricing page doesn‚Äôt require innovation; it requires
clarity.

The framework is straightforward:

  * Hero section that sets context

  * Pricing Menu that communicates essential information

  * Feature Comparison Table that removes ambiguity

  * FAQ that addresses lingering questions

If visitors can understand your pricing model and identify their fit within 30
seconds to a minute, you‚Äôve done your job.

Resist the temptation to pack your pricing page with every detail, feature,
and differentiator.

The best pricing pages respect the visitor‚Äôs time and intelligence. They
present information in a familiar structure and highlight what truly matters.

Remember: your visitors have seen this before. They know what they‚Äôre looking
for. Your job is simply to make it easy for them to find it.

Start with the basics outlined in this guide. Launch it. Gather feedback.
Iterate. A simple, clear pricing page that helps visitors make confident
decisions will always outperform a complicated one that tries to do too much.
