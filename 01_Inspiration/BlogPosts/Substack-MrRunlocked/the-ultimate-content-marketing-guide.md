Content marketing is an indiscussible channel for early-stage B2B startups. It
can drive results across the whole funnel ‚Äî brand awareness, acquire leads,
revenue, and even expansion ‚Äî while making acquisition organic and scalable,
which is what every VC seeks.

Content should be among your first channels as an early-stage SaaS. Once
you‚Äôve developed the foundation of your [product marketing
messaging](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/the-ultimate-saas-messaging-
framework), content is the first avenue to help you articulate that into a
compelling narrative.

Yes, [outbound](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/the-best-outbound-tech-stack)
should also be among your first channels, as it‚Äôs mostly free, highly
scalable, and provides faster feedback loops than content.

However, in B2B you‚Äôre selling to professionals; and no professional or
company will buy from someone they don‚Äôt trust. Content is a unique avenue
that lets you show your audience you‚Äôre an expert in what you're selling.
Differently from [ads](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/how-to-get-started-with-
linkedin) or [outbound](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/the-best-outbound-tech-
stack), content helps you project confidence in your prospects, helping them
build a relationship of affinity so that when the time is right, they‚Äôll trust
you with their money, and choose you over
[competitors](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/3-types-of-competition).

To this day, the cheapest and most scalable content starts in written form,
especially thanks to [AI which gave us ‚Äúinfinite
labor‚Äù](https://andrewchen.substack.com/p/ai-and-marketing-what-happens-
next?utm_source=post-email-
title&publication_id=2401262&post_id=144673032&utm_campaign=email-post-
title&isFreemail=true&r=2rm42&triedRedirect=true&utm_medium=email). But where
AI solves for execution, the strategy gap remains wider than ever.**** Most
startups we advise struggle with content strategy.

**Common pitfalls include:**

‚ùå Not investing in product marketing ahead of content,

‚ùå Inconsistent production and distribution,

‚ùå Over-reliance on AI-generated content that drives volume but lacks depth and
taste.

But above all, one of the biggest mistakes we see is that we need to figure
out where to start with content.

_**‚ÄúWe should talk about our vision‚Äù, ‚ÄúLet‚Äôs do a series about the key
customer pain points‚Äù, or ‚ÄúWe need to target these high-volume keywords‚Äù.**_

More often than not, these mistakes are rooted in the founders‚Äô inner
conviction and vision, because they hire junior marketers on their first
rodeo; or because even when they hire senior experts, they discard their
advice (yes, this happens all the time!).

How can your startup overcome these
[pitfalls](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/the-10-most-common-gtm-myths) and
develop an effective content marketing strategy that delivers tangible
results?

In this post, we‚Äôll go beyond the cookie-cutter advice like ‚Äúyou must know
your audience‚Äù. We‚Äôll devise a strategy process intertwined with product
marketing by defining your buyer‚Äôs stages of awareness, selecting your best
starting point for content production, and then sharing how to build robust
content programs using both human writers and AI.

Before writing content, you must invest in product marketing.

Product marketing is the strategic foundation that defines your TAM and
[target personas](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/framework-ideal-customer-
profile) (aka. Ideal Customer Profile), analyzes their goals, the
alternatives/competitors they‚Äôre using to achieve them, and the problems
arising from them; helping you ‚Äúmessage‚Äù and
‚Äú[position](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/saas-positioning-6-different-ways)‚Äù
your company as a compelling solution.

We see many founders and teams under-indexing in product marketing,
tremendously diluting their content. Product marketing comes before content;
if you skip it, you‚Äôll just have weaker messaging, weaker content, and more
confused prospects.

Now that you‚Äôve set your product marketing foundation and are ready to
confidently articulate your value proposition in a language relatable to your
ICP, you need to map your awareness stages.

The [Stages of
Awareness](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthonypierri_productmarketing-saas-
startups-activity-7198321601585569792-rHpG) is a framework built by Anthony
Pierri that helps you understand the level of awareness your customers have
towards the outcome they‚Äôre trying to achieve with your product. FYI, this is
not content, but product marketing.

For example, Anthony used the B2B gift-sending platform Sendoso to explain the
framework. Let‚Äôs go through it in a bit more detail.

At this stage,**** prospects are aware of different potential outcomes they
want to achieve. Outcomes as intended here refers to the [JTBD definition
according to Bob Moesta](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xQV7HVyAJjc) ‚Äî the
process a consumer goes through when they aim to change their existing
situation into a preferred one, but cannot do so because of
[constraints](https://www.perplexity.ai/search/what-s-the-definition-of-job-t-
M_1YeTULQQKdT5RPFVd9lw) that stop them.

In the case of Sendoso:

üëâ Maybe they want to focus on making their sales team happier at work.

üëâ Maybe they want to make their teams more productive overall.

üëâ Maybe they want to close more deals.

This is typical of a prospect that is in the earliest stages of their
purchasing decision and/or in new markets ‚Äî they know what they want to
achieve, but are not aware of how to do it or which solutions are out there.

Instead, they are battling against ‚Äúthe old/current way‚Äù of doing things,
because they‚Äôre not aware of a ‚Äúnew way‚Äù (well, that could be you!).

Once a prospect has picked their outcome, they start evaluating different
things they could do to achieve that goal. These options reflect the next
stage of awareness, where customers are shifting to explore solutions to
achieve their outcomes, according to [Anthony Ulwick‚Äôs definition of
JTBD](https://anthonyulwick.com/jobs-to-be-done/).

In the example of Sendoso:

üëâ Maybe they want to focus on training their team in new sales methodologies.

üëâ Maybe they want to create new sales enablement material.

üëâ Or maybe they want to start sending gifts to prospects during the sales
process.

Let's assume they decide to go with sending gifts to prospects as the key use
case.

Once they've chosen a use case, they need to figure out the best product
category that will allow them to send gifts to customers. They start debating
between:

üëâ eGift card platforms

üëâ Branded merch platforms

üëâ and gift-sending platforms.

Each of these product categories will help them accomplish the use case but in
very different ways, depending on their product capabilities, features, and
related benefits. This awareness stage is typical of the middle stages of the
purchasing funnel, but also of mature markets where customers are already
aware of the existing product categories to solve specific issues. Ultimately,
they decide to go with gift-sending platforms.

Now that they‚Äôve committed to a product category, they evaluate different
alternatives of gift-sending platforms to compare each feature and
functionality to see which one best fits what they need to be able to do until
they make a purchasing decision. They check out Reachdesk, Postal, and
Sendoso.

Depending on the [growth model](https://www.mrrunlocked.com/p/product-led-vs-
sales-led-saas-growth) of your company, they will:

üëâ **PLG** : sign up for trials of these products, and compare capabilities
against them.

üëâ **Sales-led** : book demos and go through the sales process to compare
pitches and prices.

After completing their research of competitive alternatives, they commit to
the purchase decision. Again, depending on the motion of your company, they
will:

üëâ **PLG** : upgrade to a paid plan.

üëâ **Sales-led** : sign a contract.

**Note** : In the real world, some stages of awareness might be mixed or have
different orders. The awareness stages, like every other framework, attempt to
make complex real-life scenarios more linear and simple. This prescribes that
each prospect is 100% clear about the outcomes they want to achieve, their use
cases, the product categories to help them do so, and each provider. However,
only some prospects have perfect clarity or perfect knowledge of the market;
and on the other hand, only some companies know how to portray themselves
against competitors. That shows how critical product marketing can be in
helping prospects to make the right choice.

By the end of this section, you should have mapped out your awareness stages
for every potential outcome your company can help with. Based on that, you can
work back the related use cases product features, and related categories.

The stages of awareness are great for mapping out your key product marketing
messages and contextualizing them to your customer journey. You can use these
not just to reverse engineer a compelling content map, but also to understand
where to start devising your content programs.

As mentioned above, most marketers and advisors will tell you:

‚ÄúStart creating BOFU (bottom of the funnel) content.‚Äù

In our opinion, it‚Äôs not that easy, but it depends on the awareness stages of
your prospect and the [maturity of your
market](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthonypierri_product-positioning-
consists-of-four-elements-
activity-7206294103263698945-F5a3?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop).

**Mature market üëâ start from Product Awareness**.

If you‚Äôre in an established market with multiple competitors, you need to
convince your market [your product is the best in your
category](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthonypierri_productmarketing-
saas-b2b-activity-7217165865434144768-iKY2/).

Start a content program around your players in your category and purchase
decisions. If your competitors have high traffic, you‚Äôll also piggyback from
prospects searching for their brand name. We‚Äôve listed below a list of content
pages you should start with. In the example of Cello, that‚Äôd mean creating
comparison pages with their competitors or boasting about why Cello is a
superior alternative for their target segments.

**Emerging market üëâ start from Product Category Awareness**.

If you‚Äôre in an emerging market and your product category is rising as a
better way to reach an outcome, you need to convince your market your product
category is the go-to alternative. For example, in the case of Cello which is
targeting founders looking to scale their organic acquisition, they might want
to create a program around why a user referral program is the best way to do
so compared to other alternatives like SEO or outbound.

**New market üëâ start from Use Case Awareness**.

If you‚Äôre building an innovative product or starting a new market, there might
be no direct competitor and you might be battling against ‚Äúthe old way of
doing things‚Äù. For example, that could be doing things on spreadsheets,
manually, etc. In this case, you should invest in creating awareness at the
higher stages, around your customer outcomes and product use cases. In this
way, you‚Äôll be able to educate your prospects about the fact that there is a
‚Äúnew way of doing things‚Äù, and you‚Äôre building it for them. For example, Cello
could create content that showcases why user referrals through an API solution
are 10x better than doing it in-house.

By following this strategy and contextualizing your content to the market,
you‚Äôll reach your target market at its current stage of awareness, speaking to
your prospects in their language, and saving countless resources on pushing
messages that are either too early or too late for the market.

Let‚Äôs continue with the Cello example. Because they operate in a fairly
competitive and mature market, I‚Äôd start creating content around the product
awareness stage, covering competitive products within the user referral
category to build a differentiating wedge with other players.

After completing one content program around this awareness stage (more on this
below), I‚Äôd move up or down the awareness stages depending on the results or
reactions around the content.
