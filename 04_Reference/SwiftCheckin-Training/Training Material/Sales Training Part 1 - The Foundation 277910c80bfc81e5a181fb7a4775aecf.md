# 1. A New Mindset for a New Era in Sales

The world of sales has fundamentally changed. Our customers, particularly busy SMB owners in the construction trades, are overwhelmed with information, constant digital distractions, and are highly skeptical of generic sales pitches. They have zero tolerance for time-wasting outreach.

**Our job is not to interrupt them, but to earn the right to their time.**

This requires a crucial mindset shift:

- **Old Model (Spray & Pray):** Pushing product features and chasing demos.
- **New Model (Problem Leadership):** Leading with empathy, understanding the customer's business, and diagnosing their specific pains.

We move from a transactional approach to a consultative, value-focused one. Our goal is to understand the customer so intimately that we can help them see the cost of inaction in their own language.

**Key Principle:** Sales today is about empathy, diagnosis, and guidance — not product pitching.

# 2. Understanding the Customer's World

## The Australian Construction Landscape

The construction industry, particularly small and mid-sized contractors, is under immense pressure. Costs of materials and wages have risen more than 30 percent since 2020. Project margins are thin. Skilled labor is hard to find. Owners are stretched between running jobs, managing cash flow, and handling compliance.

Technology adoption lags behind other industries. Many contractors still cling to Excel or paper timesheets. When they do buy software, they are skeptical and risk-averse. They don’t want “nice to have” tools. They want solutions that directly protect margin, save time, and reduce risk.

**For Swift Check-in, this is good news.** Our platform sits squarely in the “must have” zone, because it stops unbudgeted overtime and provides irrefutable subcontractor verification.

**Business Language to Master:** time, money, risk, certainty. These are the currencies of construction conversations.

## The B2B Customer Buying Journey

Let’s reflect on something familiar: the last time you bought something important for yourself. Perhaps you bought clothing for a wedding. You didn’t wake up and say, “I must buy a sari today.” An event triggered the need. You researched, asked friends, weighed price against quality, and made the decision.

Business buyers go through the same journey, but with more people involved.

The stages look like this:

1. **Awareness.** The company realizes something is wrong or could be improved.
2. **Consideration.** They evaluate possible approaches.
3. **Decision.** They select a vendor and sign.
4. **Activation.** They implement the solution.

Your work as an SDR lives in the **Awareness** stage. Here, your job is to help the buyer move from “I don’t have a problem” to “Yes, this is a problem and I should evaluate solutions.”

# 3. The Five Stages of Prospect Awareness

To guide a prospect effectively, we must first diagnose where they are in their journey. People rarely skip stages.

1. **Problem Unaware:** They don't realize they have a problem. They see issues like minor overtime costs as "just the cost of doing business."
2. **Problem Aware:** They feel the pain but don't know a solution exists. (e.g., "Chasing timesheets every Friday is a nightmare, but I guess that's just how it is.")
3. **Solution Aware:** They know solutions exist but are unsure which is right for them. (e.g., "I know there are apps for this, but which one works with my accounting software?")
4. **Product Aware:** They know about specific products (like ours) but haven't decided if it's the right fit or if the timing is right.
5. **Buyer Intent:** They are actively searching for and comparing solutions now.

**Your most fertile ground:** those who are **Problem Aware**. These buyers already feel the pain, and you can lead them to a solution.

# 5. The SPICED Framework: Our Diagnostic Tool

SPICED is a structured questioning process to help us and the prospect fully understand their challenges, creating an internal motivation for change.

**S - Situation:** Objective facts about their current operations. (e.g., How do you currently collect timesheets?)

**P - Pain:** The frustrations and inefficiencies associated with the situation. (e.g., What's the most frustrating part of chasing the crew for timesheets every Friday?)

**I - Impact:** The quantifiable business consequences of the pain. This is the most critical step. It links the pain to time, money, or risk. (e.g., How does that delay impact payroll accuracy or your ability to invoice clients on time?)

**CE - Critical Event:** A specific deadline or future event that creates urgency. (e.g., You mentioned hiring two new staff members next month. How will your current process handle that increase?)

**D - Decision:** Understanding their internal buying process. (e.g., Who else besides yourself would need to be involved in evaluating a new system like this?)

# 6. From Research to Outreach

Effective outreach is built on a foundation of fast, disciplined research. The goal is to gather just enough information to form a credible hypothesis about the prospect's Situation and Pain.

## Research Process:

1. **Company Profile:** What is their trade, size, and location?
2. **Timing Triggers:** Are they hiring? Have they won new projects? Is there a relevant weather event?
3. **Key Stakeholders:** Identify the Owner, Ops Manager, and Admin on their website or LinkedIn.
4. **Formulate a Hypothesis:** Create an educated guess about their primary pain point.

This allows you to craft a personalised, relevant, and compelling message that cuts through the noise.

## Example Outreach Angle:

> "Hi Mr. Customer, I saw on LinkedIn you're hiring five new carpenters for the new apartment project. With added staff on a major project, many directors find it's a critical time to ensure overtime doesn't eat into the job's profitability. How are you planning to track labor costs in real-time for that project?"
